# FISA 702 Renewal Could Strip VPN Users of Constitutional Protections

**TL;DR:** US surveillance law renewal may classify VPN traffic as 'foreign' by default, potentially subjecting American VPN users to warrantless surveillance under Section 702.

## Key Points

- Section 702 of FISA allows warrantless surveillance of 'foreign' communications, routinely capturing Americans' data
- VPN traffic routing through foreign servers may be classified as 'foreign' under current surveillance rules
- Classification could strip VPN users of constitutional protections and place them in Section 702 pipeline
- Expanded definition of 'electronic communication service providers' now includes cloud services, WiFi networks
- Senators Ron Wyden and Mike Lee propose Government Surveillance Reform Act requiring warrants for Americans' data
- Bill would close loophole allowing government to buy personal data from brokers without court oversight
- Bipartisan opposition includes protests at House Intelligence Committee Democrat Jim Himes' town halls
- April deadline approaching for Section 702 renewal with minimal proposed changes

## Deep Dive

## The Technical Loophole

VPNs work by routing traffic through servers worldwide to obscure user location and encrypt data. However, this fundamental privacy-protecting behavior creates a classification problem under FISA 702. When American traffic passes through foreign infrastructure, surveillance systems may treat it as 'foreign communication' subject to warrantless collection. This represents a direct threat to millions of VPN users who adopted the technology specifically to protect their privacy.

## Expanding Surveillance Infrastructure

The expanded definition of 'electronic communication service providers' (ECSPs) fundamentally changes the surveillance landscape. Previously limited to telecoms and email providers, the definition now encompasses any entity with access to data systems - cloud services, public WiFi operators, and potentially VPN providers themselves. This creates multiple collection points where VPN traffic could be intercepted before encryption or after decryption.

## Legislative Response and Timeline

The Government Surveillance Reform Act offers comprehensive reforms including warrant requirements for searching Americans' data and closing the data broker loophole that allows purchasing location and browsing data without judicial oversight. However, with an April renewal deadline and bipartisan support for extending current powers, meaningful reform faces significant political obstacles. The technical reality of modern internet infrastructure makes the foreign/domestic distinction increasingly meaningless, yet the legal framework hasn't adapted.

## Implications for Privacy Tools

This development signals a broader threat to privacy tools beyond VPNs. Any technology that routes traffic internationally or obscures user location could face similar classification issues. The precedent could extend to Tor, mesh networks, or other censorship-resistant technologies that rely on distributed infrastructure crossing national boundaries.
