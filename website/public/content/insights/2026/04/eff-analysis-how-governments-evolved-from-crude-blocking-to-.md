# EFF Analysis: How Governments Evolved From Crude Blocking to Sophisticated Censorship Systems

**TL;DR:** EFF documents how government censorship evolved from simple website blocks that VPNs could bypass to sophisticated platform control systems, using Egypt's transformation from Arab Spring catalyst to digital dystopia as a case study.

## Key Points

- 66% of internet users now live where political/social sites are blocked, up from crude blocking 15 years ago
- 78% are in countries where people face arrest for online posts, with new social media regulations in dozens of countries
- Egypt went from Facebook-sparked revolution in 2011 to imprisoning digital rights defenders under broad cybercrime laws
- 2018 Egyptian Cybercrime Law allows website blocking for vague 'national security' threats, criminalizes accessing banned sites
- Articles 25-26 used to prosecute social media users for 'violating family values' - many targets are young women
- Governments shifted from reactive blocking to proactive platform pressure, making VPN circumvention less effective
- Early 2000s government responses (Thailand blocking YouTube, Turkey takedown demands) were testing grounds for current systems

## Deep Dive

## The Evolution of Digital Authoritarianism

This EFF analysis provides crucial documentation of how government censorship tactics have evolved from the crude website blocking of the early 2000s to today's sophisticated systems of platform control. The transformation is stark: where activists once could reliably use VPNs to bypass government blocks, today's censorship operates through legal frameworks that make platforms themselves instruments of state control.

## Egypt as a Case Study in Digital Regression

Egypt's trajectory is particularly instructive for freedom tech builders. The same country where Facebook's "We Are All Khaled Said" page helped spark revolution now imprisons people for accessing blocked websites. The 2018 Cybercrime Law exemplifies how vague definitions of "national security" and "family values" create broad authority for suppression. Notably, the law doesn't just target content creators—accessing banned sites can result in six months imprisonment, making the stakes much higher for users of circumvention tools.

## Technical and Legal Sophistication

The shift from blocking entire platforms to pressuring them for selective content removal represents a fundamental change in the threat model. Early censorship was binary—sites were either accessible or not. Today's systems create compliance frameworks where platforms become active participants in censorship, making technical circumvention more complex and legally riskier for users.

## Implications for Builders

This evolution suggests that freedom tech solutions must account for both technical sophistication and legal pressure. Simple proxy tools are insufficient when governments can criminalize access itself and when platforms face systematic pressure to comply with content removal demands. The focus must shift toward solutions that provide both technical robustness and legal protection for users.
