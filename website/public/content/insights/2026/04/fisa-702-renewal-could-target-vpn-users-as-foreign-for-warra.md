# FISA 702 Renewal Could Target VPN Users as 'Foreign' for Warrantless Surveillance

**TL;DR:** Section 702 renewal debate reveals VPN users may be classified as 'foreign' targets, subjecting Americans to warrantless surveillance simply for using privacy tools.

## Key Points

- Congress debating renewal of FISA Section 702 with April deadline approaching
- VPN traffic routing through foreign servers may trigger 'foreign' classification under Section 702
- Americans using VPNs could lose constitutional protections and face warrantless surveillance
- Expanded definition of 'electronic communication service providers' forces more entities to assist surveillance
- Bipartisan Government Surveillance Reform Act proposes warrant requirements and closes data broker loophole
- Current law allows purchasing personal data from brokers instead of obtaining warrants
- Sen. Ron Wyden and others questioning whether intelligence agencies default-classify VPN traffic as foreign
- Proton warns that privacy tools paradoxically increase surveillance risk under current legal framework

## Deep Dive

## Legal Framework Creating Privacy Tool Penalties

Section 702 of FISA was designed for foreign intelligence collection but routinely captures Americans' communications through global internet infrastructure. The law's renewal debate has exposed a critical flaw: VPNs, which route traffic through international servers to protect privacy, may trigger automatic classification as 'foreign' communications, stripping users of Fourth Amendment protections. This creates a perverse incentive where using privacy tools increases surveillance exposure.

## Technical and Legal Expansion of Surveillance Reach

Recent expansions to the definition of 'electronic communication service providers' have dramatically widened the surveillance net beyond traditional telecoms and email providers. Cloud services, public WiFi networks, and potentially any entity with access to internet infrastructure can now be compelled to assist in surveillance operations. This infrastructure-level approach means data collection points have multiplied throughout the internet's backbone, making it nearly impossible for users to avoid potential collection.

## Reform Proposals and Their Limitations

The bipartisan Government Surveillance Reform Act addresses some concerns by requiring warrants for searching Americans' data and closing the data broker loophole that allows purchasing information that would otherwise require court approval. However, the fundamental tension between privacy tool use and foreign intelligence authorities remains unresolved. Even with reforms, the technical reality of global internet routing means privacy-conscious Americans may continue facing elevated surveillance risk simply for protecting their communications.

## Implications for Freedom Tech Builders

This legal framework creates serious considerations for privacy tool developers. Tools that route traffic internationally or obscure user location may inadvertently increase users' surveillance exposure under current law. The debate highlights the need for both technical solutions that maintain privacy without triggering foreign intelligence collection and continued advocacy for legal frameworks that don't penalize privacy-protective behavior.
