# EFF Documents Evolution of Digital Censorship Since Arab Spring

**TL;DR:** EFF analysis shows how governments transformed from crude internet blocks to sophisticated censorship systems targeting platforms directly, making simple circumvention tools less effective.

## Key Points

- EFF published second part of series examining digital legacy of 2011 Arab uprisings
- 66% of internet users now live where political/social sites are blocked according to Freedom House
- 78% are in countries where people have been arrested for online posts
- Pre-2011: Governments used crude blocks that VPNs could easily bypass
- Post-2011: Governments shifted to pressuring platforms into compliance and sophisticated control systems
- Egypt exemplifies regression - went from Facebook-sparked revolution to comprehensive digital repression
- Egypt's 2018 Cybercrime Law criminalizes accessing banned sites (up to 6 months imprisonment)
- Vague 'national security' and 'public morals' provisions used to prosecute social media users
- Pattern replicated across Russia, Nigeria, Turkey with platform-targeted regulations

## Deep Dive

## The Sophistication of Modern Censorship

This EFF analysis reveals a critical evolution in the threat landscape that freedom tech builders must understand. The shift from crude website blocks (easily bypassed by VPNs) to sophisticated platform pressure represents a fundamental change in how authoritarian governments approach information control.

## Technical and Legal Convergence

The Egyptian case study demonstrates how legal frameworks now complement technical censorship. The 2018 Cybercrime Law doesn't just block content—it criminalizes access itself, creating legal jeopardy for users attempting circumvention. Articles 25 and 26's vague language around 'family values' and 'public morals' create broad prosecutorial discretion that chills speech even when technical circumvention is possible.

## Platform Compliance as Control Infrastructure

The evolution from blocking YouTube entirely (as Turkey did in 2007) to pressuring platforms for selective takedowns represents a more effective control strategy. Governments discovered that platform compliance is more scalable and harder to circumvent than network-level blocking.

## Implications for Freedom Tech

This analysis suggests that effective circumvention tools must address both technical and legal attack vectors. Simple VPN-style solutions that worked in 2011 are insufficient against modern censorship regimes that combine platform pressure, legal criminalization of access, and sophisticated content filtering. The freedom tech community needs solutions that provide both technical anonymity and legal deniability.
