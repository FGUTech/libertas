# South Sudan's New Cybercrime Law Criminalizes Digital Speech with 10-Year Prison Terms

**TL;DR:** South Sudan's 2026 Cybercrimes Act criminalizes 'false information' and 'undesirable content' with up to 10 years prison, grants authorities broad surveillance powers, and threatens encrypted communications and journalism.

## Key Points

- President Salva Kiir signed the Cybercrimes and Computer Misuse Act 2026 on February 18, replacing 2021 provisional order
- Law punishes defamation with up to 5 years prison, increasing from previous 2-year maximum under penal code
- Vague definitions of 'false or misleading information' and 'undesirable content' enable subjective interpretation by authorities
- Section 6(3) grants National Communication Authority power to access 'any' data from ISPs without judicial oversight
- Section 33 criminalizes unauthorized data transmission with 5-year penalty, no exemptions for whistleblowing or investigative journalism
- Platform administrators face 5 years prison for failing to remove broadly-defined 'unlawful content'
- 'Cyberbullying' carries up to 10-year prison sentence under expansive definition covering digital harassment
- Committee to Protect Journalists calls for urgent reform, citing threats to press freedom and legitimate online activity
- Pattern matches regional trend of cybercrime laws being used to suppress journalism across Africa

## Deep Dive

## Context and Regional Pattern

South Sudan's 2026 Cybercrimes Act represents a significant escalation in digital authoritarianism, occurring against a backdrop of poor press freedom since 2011 independence. The CPJ has documented at least 10 journalists killed since 2012, with authorities previously suspending social media and shuttering outlets like Hot in Juba [Source]. This law codifies digital repression while many African nations are moving in the opposite direction—11 countries have abolished criminal defamation laws since 2001.

## Technical and Surveillance Implications

The law's technical provisions pose severe threats to freedom tech infrastructure. Section 6(3) grants the National Communication Authority unprecedented surveillance powers, allowing warrantless access to ISP data deemed 'important for protection of users' without judicial oversight [Source]. Section 33's criminalization of 'unauthorized data transmission' with no public interest exemptions directly threatens encrypted communications, Tor usage, and secure messaging apps that rely on routing data through unauthorized channels.

## Threat Model for Freedom Tech

The law's vague language creates a legal framework for prosecuting virtually any digital activity. 'Undesirable content' includes information that 'threatens national security' or 'promotes tribalism'—terms that could encompass everything from encrypted messaging to decentralized networks [Source]. The 10-year maximum for 'cyberbullying' (defined as 'hurtful, targeted behavior' using digital technologies) could criminalize activism, protest coordination, or even privacy advocacy.

## Builder Considerations

This represents a new model of comprehensive digital control through legal ambiguity. The law's broad definitions and severe penalties create chilling effects that extend beyond journalism to any form of digital dissent or privacy-seeking behavior. For freedom tech builders, this demonstrates how authoritarian governments are evolving beyond technical censorship to legal frameworks that make the mere use of privacy tools potentially criminal.
