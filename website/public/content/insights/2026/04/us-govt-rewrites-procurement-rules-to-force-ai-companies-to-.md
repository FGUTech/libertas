# US Govt Rewrites Procurement Rules to Force AI Companies to Disable Safety Guardrails

**TL;DR:** GSA proposes procurement rules requiring AI contractors to license systems for 'all lawful purposes' and disable safety guardrails that might refuse government requests, effectively weaponizing contracts against AI safety.

## Key Points

- General Services Administration proposes new procurement rules targeting AI contractors
- Requirements include licensing AI systems to government for 'all lawful purposes'
- Companies must disable safety guardrails that could refuse government requests
- Rules emerge amid DOD-Anthropic dispute over AI use for mass surveillance
- EFF and coalition filed comments opposing rules as threat to AI safety
- Proposal includes 'anti-Woke' requirements described as technologically incoherent
- Rules would become standard components of every federal contract if adopted
- Government using procurement power to circumvent direct regulatory resistance

## Deep Dive

## Context and Background

These procurement rule changes represent a significant escalation in government efforts to access AI capabilities without safety restrictions. The timing is not coincidental—this proposal emerges during an ongoing dispute between the Department of Defense and Anthropic, where the company has refused to allow its technology to be used for mass surveillance [Source]. Rather than engage in public battles with individual companies, the government is attempting to preemptively eliminate such resistance through contract terms.

## Technical and Policy Implications

The 'all lawful purposes' licensing requirement creates a massive loophole for government surveillance expansion. Given the government's documented history of loose legal interpretations, data broker collaborations, and willingness to engage in illegal spying, this effectively means no practical limits on AI system usage [Source]. The requirement to disable safety guardrails is particularly concerning—it forces companies to remove protections they've deemed necessary, potentially creating systemic risks.

## Threat Model Analysis

This represents a new vector of regulatory capture—using procurement power to achieve what direct regulation might not. Companies face a stark choice: either comply with these terms and lose safety controls, or forfeit all federal contracts. For many AI companies, federal contracts represent significant revenue streams, making this economic coercion particularly effective.

## What Builders Should Consider

The proposal demonstrates how governments can leverage economic dependencies to override technical safety decisions. Builders should consider diversifying revenue sources away from government contracts and designing systems with safety guardrails that cannot be easily disabled through contract terms. The coalition opposition suggests there may be legal challenges, but builders shouldn't rely on regulatory protection alone.
