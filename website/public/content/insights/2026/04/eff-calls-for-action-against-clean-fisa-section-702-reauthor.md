# EFF Calls for Action Against Clean FISA Section 702 Reauthorization

**TL;DR:** EFF is mobilizing opposition to a 'clean' reauthorization of FISA Section 702, which allows warrantless surveillance of Americans' communications with foreign persons overseas.

## Key Points

- FISA Section 702 is up for congressional reauthorization with April 20 deadline
- Current law allows NSA to collect Americans' communications with foreign persons without warrants
- FBI operates in 'finders keepers' mode, querying stored communications without warrants
- Surveillance targets have no notification and limited ways to discover they've been spied on
- EFF opposes any 'clean' reauthorization without substantial privacy reforms
- Intelligence community prioritizes surveillance access over privacy protections
- Civil liberties groups have fought for years to expose secretive court rulings on 702 usage

## Deep Dive

## Context and Background

Section 702 of the Foreign Intelligence Surveillance Act represents one of the most significant warrantless surveillance authorities in U.S. law. The provision allows intelligence agencies to collect communications of foreign targets overseas, but inevitably sweeps up communications involving Americans. This creates a legal loophole where agencies can access American communications without obtaining traditional FISA warrants.

## Technical and Legal Framework

The surveillance operates through a two-step process: the NSA collects and stores full conversations involving overseas targets, then the FBI queries this database for U.S. person communications without additional legal process. This 'backdoor search' capability effectively circumvents Fourth Amendment protections, as Americans have no knowledge of the surveillance and limited legal recourse.

## Threat Model Implications

The lack of warrant requirements creates a massive privacy vulnerability for anyone communicating internationally. The system operates with minimal oversight and no notification requirements, making it impossible for affected individuals to challenge surveillance in court. This undermines fundamental principles of due process and judicial oversight that normally constrain government surveillance powers.

## What Builders Should Consider

The EFF's mobilization suggests significant political momentum against surveillance overreach. Privacy-focused builders should monitor this legislative battle as it will set precedents for surveillance authorities and digital rights protections. The outcome will signal whether Congress is willing to impose meaningful constraints on intelligence agencies or will continue expanding surveillance powers without corresponding privacy safeguards.
