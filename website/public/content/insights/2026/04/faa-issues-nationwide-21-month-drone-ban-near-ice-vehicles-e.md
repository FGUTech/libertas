# FAA Issues Nationwide 21-Month Drone Ban Near ICE Vehicles, EFF Challenges

**TL;DR:** Trump administration issued unprecedented nationwide flight restriction banning drones within half-mile of ICE/CBP vehicles for 21 months, effectively criminalizing accountability journalism.

## Key Points

- FAA issued TFR FDC 6/4375 on January 16, 2026, lasting until October 29, 2027 - 21 months total
- Restriction covers entire US, bans drones within 3000 feet of any DHS/DOJ/DOD/DOE vehicles including ICE/CBP
- Violators face criminal charges, civil penalties, and drone seizure/destruction
- EFF and major news organizations demanded FAA lift restriction in January, no response after 2+ months
- ICE often uses unmarked rental cars and swaps license plates, making compliance impossible
- Timing coincides with Minneapolis anti-ICE protests and killings of Renée Good and Alex Pretti
- EFF argues violation of First Amendment right to record police and Fifth Amendment due process
- FAA failed to specify hazard justification or provide media contact as required by own regulations

## Deep Dive

## Context and Unprecedented Scope

This TFR represents an extraordinary expansion of flight restrictions both in duration and geographic scope. While TFRs typically last hours and cover specific locations during events or disasters, FDC 6/4375 spans 21 months across the entire United States. The restriction's timing—beginning January 16, 2026 during heightened ICE enforcement and Minneapolis protests—suggests deliberate suppression of accountability journalism rather than legitimate safety concerns.

## Technical and Legal Implications

The 3000-foot horizontal restriction creates an effective half-mile no-fly zone around any federal law enforcement vehicle. This is particularly problematic given ICE's documented use of unmarked rental cars, vehicles without license plates, and frequent license plate swapping to avoid detection [Source]. Drone operators cannot comply with restrictions they cannot identify, yet face criminal prosecution and equipment destruction for violations. The FAA's failure to specify the underlying hazard or provide media exemption procedures violates the agency's own regulations requiring justification and press access.

## Constitutional Vulnerabilities

The restriction directly conflicts with established First Amendment precedent recognizing the right to record law enforcement, as affirmed by nearly every federal appeals court. The cases of George Floyd, Renée Good, and Alex Pretti demonstrate how civilian recordings expose false official narratives and drive accountability. By criminalizing this documentation, the TFR creates a systematic shield against oversight. The Fifth Amendment due process violation is equally clear—operators face punishment without fair notice of restricted areas.

## Strategic Implications for Freedom Tech

This case represents a critical test of aerial surveillance and accountability tools under authoritarian pressure. The government's willingness to abuse aviation regulations to suppress journalism suggests similar restrictions could target other freedom tech applications. The lack of FAA response to EFF's formal challenge indicates potential administrative resistance to legal oversight. Success in overturning this restriction could establish important precedent for drone-based accountability journalism and citizen surveillance of government operations.
