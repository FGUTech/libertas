# New Mexico Court Ruling Uses E2E Encryption Implementation as Evidence Against Meta

**TL;DR:** New Mexico court ruling against Meta cited the company's 2023 addition of end-to-end encryption to Messenger as evidence of negligent design, potentially setting precedent that could discourage encryption deployment.

## Key Points

- New Mexico AG used Meta's 2023 E2E encryption rollout for Messenger as key evidence of negligent design
- State argued encryption 'shielded bad actors' and hampered law enforcement access to evidence
- Court may order Meta to weaken encryption protections under 'design liability' theory
- Ruling creates dangerous precedent where privacy-protective features become litigation evidence
- Internal company safety discussions also used as 'smoking gun' evidence against Meta
- Legal framework incentivizes companies to avoid deploying security improvements
- Schneier warns this could discourage internal risk assessments and safety deliberations

## Deep Dive

## Legal Precedent Threat

The New Mexico ruling represents a fundamental shift in how courts may evaluate privacy-protective technologies. By treating Meta's implementation of end-to-end encryption as evidence of negligent design, the court has established a framework where security improvements can be weaponized in litigation. This 'design liability' theory directly contradicts decades of security expert consensus that strong encryption protects far more people from harm than it enables.

## Technical and Policy Implications

The ruling's logic creates a perverse incentive structure for technology companies. If deploying encryption can become 'Exhibit A' in future lawsuits, rational corporate actors will simply avoid implementing these protections. This extends beyond encryption to any privacy-preserving feature that could theoretically be exploited by bad actors - essentially all of them. The court's reasoning ignores that encryption is content-neutral technology, no different from postal mail or telephone systems.

## Corporate Response Patterns

Perhaps more concerning is how internal company safety discussions were weaponized as evidence. When good-faith engineering deliberations about security tradeoffs become courtroom ammunition, companies will predictably stop documenting these critical conversations. This 'ignorance is safer than inquiry' dynamic will lead to worse security outcomes as organizations avoid the kind of rigorous internal debate that produces robust systems.

## Broader Freedom Tech Impact

This case could chill development across the entire freedom tech ecosystem. If major platforms face legal liability for deploying standard cryptographic protections, smaller projects building explicitly censorship-resistant tools face even greater legal risk. The ruling's precedential value may extend beyond civil litigation into regulatory enforcement, creating a pathway for governments to attack encryption deployment through the court system rather than direct legislation.
