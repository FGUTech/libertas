# Gabon Blocks Social Media Platforms During Anti-Government Protests

**TL;DR:** Gabon's government indefinitely blocked Facebook, WhatsApp, TikTok, Instagram, and YouTube during ongoing protests, with 345+ rights organizations demanding immediate restoration of access.

## Key Points

- Jean-Claude Mendome of Gabon's High Authority for Communication announced indefinite social media shutdown on Feb 17, 2026
- OONI confirmed blocking of Facebook, WhatsApp, TikTok, Instagram, and YouTube as of Feb 18, 2026
- Shutdown coincides with anti-government protests and labor strikes across the country
- Government claims blocks prevent 'spread of false information' and protect national unity
- 345+ human rights organizations from #KeepItOn coalition demand immediate access restoration
- Gabon has pattern of shutdowns: 2023 elections, 2021 COVID protests, 2019 attempted coup, 2016 post-election
- Coalition calls on ISPs (Moov Gabon, Gabon Telecom, Airtel Gabon) to refuse government shutdown orders
- Violations cited include Gabonese Constitution Article 1(2) and international human rights frameworks

## Deep Dive

## Context and Pattern

Gabon's latest social media shutdown represents a continuation of systematic digital repression dating back to 2016. The timing during anti-government protests follows the established authoritarian playbook of information control during political instability. Previous shutdowns during the 2023 elections (lasting until a military coup) and 2021 COVID protests demonstrate this is not an isolated incident but part of broader digital authoritarianism.

## Technical Implementation

OONI's network measurement data confirms platform-level blocking rather than complete internet shutdown, suggesting sophisticated deep packet inspection or DNS filtering. The targeting of specific platforms (Facebook, WhatsApp, TikTok, Instagram, YouTube) indicates authorities are attempting to disrupt protest coordination while maintaining some economic internet functions. This selective approach represents an evolution in censorship tactics.

## Resistance Framework

The #KeepItOn coalition's response highlights the growing institutionalized resistance to internet shutdowns. With 345+ organizations from 105 countries, this represents significant coordinated pressure combining legal frameworks (African Charter on Human and Peoples' Rights, ICCPR), technical documentation (OONI measurements), and direct ISP pressure. The explicit naming of telecom providers (Moov Gabon, Gabon Telecom, Airtel Gabon) creates accountability pressure at the implementation level.

## Implications for Freedom Tech

This case demonstrates both the vulnerability of centralized platforms and the importance of technical documentation in resistance efforts. The selective blocking suggests circumvention tools targeting specific platforms could be effective, while the coalition's coordinated response shows the value of institutional networks in documenting and responding to digital repression.
