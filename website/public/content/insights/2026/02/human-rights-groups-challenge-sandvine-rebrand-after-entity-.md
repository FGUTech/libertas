# Human Rights Groups Challenge Sandvine Rebrand After Entity List Removal

**TL;DR:** Access Now demands AppLogic Networks (formerly Sandvine) prove its surveillance tech reforms after U.S. removed company from Entity List, citing lack of transparency on Egypt exit and failure to remedy DPI abuses.

## Key Points

- U.S. Commerce Dept removed AppLogic Networks (formerly Sandvine) from Entity List in October 2024 citing announced reforms
- Company was blacklisted in February 2024 for enabling Egyptian mass surveillance via deep packet inspection (DPI) technology
- DPI equipment was used for web monitoring, censorship, and spyware injection against dissidents and human rights defenders
- AppLogic committed to exiting Egypt by December 31, 2025 and consulting with civil society groups
- Access Now reports company has failed to engage with Egyptian civil society despite outreach attempts
- No transparent confirmation provided of complete Egypt exit or remediation plans for remaining equipment
- Human rights groups warn Entity List removal based on PR statements rather than verifiable actions
- Case highlights fragility of accountability mechanisms for surveillance tech companies

## Deep Dive

## Corporate Accountability Theater

The AppLogic Networks case represents a critical test of whether surveillance technology companies can escape accountability through superficial reforms. The company's addition to the U.S. Entity List in February 2024 followed documented evidence of its DPI technology enabling Egyptian authorities to conduct mass web monitoring and inject spyware against government critics [Source]. Within months, announced corporate reforms and a rebrand were sufficient to secure removal from the blacklist.

## Technical and Strategic Implications

Sandvine's DPI technology represents the backbone of internet censorship infrastructure in authoritarian regimes. The technology enables real-time analysis and manipulation of internet traffic at the packet level, allowing for targeted surveillance, content blocking, and malware injection. The company's commitment to exit Egypt by end of 2025 raises critical questions about the fate of existing surveillance infrastructure and whether operators can simply transition to alternative vendors.

## Precedent for Freedom Tech

This case establishes concerning precedent where surveillance tech companies can potentially escape consequences through public relations campaigns rather than substantive reform. For freedom tech builders, this underscores the importance of designing systems that are resilient against DPI-based attacks and highlights the limitations of relying on government sanctions as a primary accountability mechanism. The failure to require verifiable remediation also signals that affected communities may need to pursue alternative forms of accountability and technical countermeasures.
