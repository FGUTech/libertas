# Wisconsin Removes VPN Ban from Age Verification Bill After Pushback

**TL;DR:** Wisconsin lawmakers removed VPN bans from age verification bill S.B. 130/A.B. 105 after widespread opposition, though EFF still opposes the bill awaiting Governor Evers' signature.

## Key Points

- Wisconsin removed VPN ban provision from S.B. 130/A.B. 105 on February 25, 2026 after pushback
- Bill still requires invasive age verification for websites with content deemed 'sexual'
- EFF continues opposing the bill despite VPN provision removal
- Original bill would have forced websites to block all VPN users to comply with Wisconsin law
- Technical impossibility of geo-blocking VPN users would lead to over-blocking or nationwide restrictions
- Bill expands definition of 'harmful to minors' beyond historically narrow court-recognized categories
- Age verification requirements create privacy nightmare requiring government IDs and biometric data
- Governor Tony Evers urged to veto the bill despite VPN provision removal

## Deep Dive

## Context and Background

This represents a significant partial victory for digital rights advocates, demonstrating that coordinated opposition can force lawmakers to retreat from the most egregious privacy violations. The original Wisconsin bill represented one of the most direct attacks on VPN usage in US state legislation, combining age verification mandates with explicit VPN blocking requirements.

## Technical and Legal Implications

The removed VPN provision highlighted fundamental technical realities that lawmakers failed to understand. Websites cannot reliably determine VPN users' actual locations, creating an impossible compliance scenario. To avoid liability, sites would either need to block all VPN traffic (affecting users globally), implement overly broad IP blocking, or restrict all Wisconsin access entirely. This technical impossibility would have effectively created nationwide VPN restrictions for compliance purposes.

## Remaining Threats

Despite the VPN provision removal, the core age verification infrastructure remains problematic. The bill still mandates collection of sensitive personal data (government IDs, financial information, biometric identifiers) and expands 'harmful to minors' definitions beyond traditional adult content to include basic sex education or anatomical information. This creates both surveillance infrastructure and over-censorship risks.

## Strategic Considerations for Builders

This case demonstrates that even partial legislative victories require sustained pressure through the entire process. The pattern of age verification bills serving as vehicles for broader internet restrictions is accelerating across multiple states. Builders should consider how compliance systems designed for one jurisdiction's requirements may create broader access restrictions, and prepare technical approaches that resist over-broad implementation of geographically-specific mandates.
