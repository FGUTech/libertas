# Florida Proposes State Spy Unit to 'Neutralize' People Based on Views and Opinions

**TL;DR:** Florida legislature advances bill creating state counterintelligence unit that could target individuals whose 'views or opinions are a threat' to the state, raising COINTELPRO-style surveillance concerns.

## Key Points

- Florida Rep. Danny Alvarez (R) sponsors bill creating state counterintelligence unit within Florida Department of Law Enforcement
- Unit empowered to 'neutralize' people whose 'demonstrated actions, views, or opinions are a threat' to Florida
- Bill passed three House committees with Senate companion pending, making passage likely
- Israeli spyware company Cellebrite tracking bill through registered lobbyist [Source]
- No explicit protections for U.S. citizens; language allows targeting based on views deemed 'inimical' to state interests
- CAIR-Florida expects Muslims to be primary targets, citing recent DeSantis designation of CAIR as 'foreign terrorist organization'
- Civil rights advocates compare to FBI's COINTELPRO program that illegally surveilled activists
- Bill includes vague 'tradecraft' language and broad definition of adversary entities including 'any person' with threatening views

## Deep Dive

## Context and Precedent

This legislation represents a significant expansion of state-level surveillance capabilities, echoing the FBI's COINTELPRO program that illegally targeted civil rights activists, anti-war protesters, and political dissidents from 1956-1971. The bill's language allowing targeting based on 'views or opinions' directly contradicts First Amendment protections and the intelligence community reforms implemented after the 1970s Church Committee investigations exposed widespread surveillance abuses.

## Technical and Legal Implications

The involvement of Cellebrite—known for providing phone-cracking technology to law enforcement—suggests the unit would deploy sophisticated digital surveillance tools. The bill's broad definition of 'adversary intelligence entities' could encompass virtually any individual or organization whose views conflict with state policy. The absence of explicit citizen protections and the inclusion of 'tradecraft' language indicates this unit would operate with minimal oversight using intelligence community methods typically reserved for foreign threats.

## Threat Model Assessment

This creates a state-sanctioned framework for political surveillance that could rapidly expand beyond initial targets. The timing coincides with Florida's designation of CAIR as a 'foreign terrorist organization,' suggesting a deliberate strategy to institutionalize religious and political persecution. For freedom tech builders, this represents a critical test case—if Florida successfully implements domestic intelligence operations at the state level, other states may follow suit, creating a patchwork of surveillance regimes that could circumvent federal oversight mechanisms.

## Strategic Implications for Builders

The legislation demonstrates how surveillance infrastructure can be rapidly institutionalized through state-level action, potentially creating a template for other jurisdictions. The lack of explicit technical constraints in the bill suggests broad surveillance authorities that could include digital monitoring, infiltration, and disruption activities traditionally associated with federal intelligence agencies.
