# Ukraine: 10 Investigative Journalists Surveilled by Criminal Network Tied to Government Officials

**TL;DR:** Ukrainian authorities are investigating surveillance of 10 corruption journalists by a criminal network including government officials, discovered during a 15-month anti-corruption probe called 'Operation Midas'.

## Key Points

- National Anti-Corruption Bureau (NABU) uncovered surveillance of 10 Ukrainian journalists covering corruption during 'Operation Midas' investigation
- Criminal network allegedly included government officials and businessmen who embezzled energy sector funds
- Journalists' personal information found on seized digital devices alongside data on politicians and security officials
- Committee to Protect Journalists calls for swift investigation into privacy violations
- Nine journalist names publicly released, including editors from Forbes Ukraine, Censor.NET, and investigative outlet Nashi Groshi
- Two of the surveilled journalists are now deceased
- Pattern follows January 2024 case where 30 SBU officers spied on Bihus.Info journalists
- Highlights operational security vulnerabilities for investigative journalists in conflict zones

## Deep Dive

## Context and Background

This surveillance case emerged from NABU's 15-month 'Operation Midas' investigation into energy sector embezzlement, revealing how corruption networks systematically monitor their critics. The targeting of investigative journalists alongside politicians and security officials suggests coordinated intelligence gathering by criminal organizations with government ties. This occurs against the backdrop of Russia's invasion, where Ukrainian journalists already face heightened risks while covering sensitive topics like corruption.

## Surveillance Infrastructure Implications

The discovery of journalists' personal data on seized digital devices indicates sophisticated data collection capabilities by non-state actors working with government insiders. The fact that this network could access personal information on journalists, NABU employees, and SBU personnel suggests either compromised official databases or extensive social engineering operations. The withholding of the tenth journalist's name due to "investigation sensitivity" implies ongoing operational security concerns.

## Threat Model for Investigative Journalists

This case demonstrates that journalists covering corruption face surveillance not just from state actors, but from hybrid criminal-government networks with significant resources. The targeting pattern shows these networks monitor journalists proactively, not just reactively to specific stories. For investigative journalists, this means assuming comprehensive digital surveillance and the need for advanced operational security practices, especially when covering stories involving government contracts or energy sector dealings.

## What Builders Should Consider

Journalists in similar contexts need secure communication tools that can resist both state-level and criminal surveillance capabilities. The multi-layered threat model requires solutions that protect against compromised government databases, social engineering, and criminal networks with technical capabilities. Tools must account for journalists operating in active conflict zones where traditional security assumptions may not apply.
