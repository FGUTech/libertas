# Uganda Faces Election Internet Shutdown Threat Despite Coalition Warning

**TL;DR:** Digital rights coalition warns Uganda's government against internet shutdowns ahead of 2026 elections, citing escalating media crackdown and concerning signals including VPN threats and Starlink blocking directive.

## Key Points

- 345-org #KeepItOn coalition appeals to President Museveni to maintain internet access during January 15, 2026 elections
- Uganda Communications Commission (UCC) threatened VPN users accessing blocked Facebook platform
- UCC directed Starlink to disable services weeks before elections, raising connectivity concerns
- Electoral Commission officials suggested internet shutdown may be necessary due to 'uncontrollable' misinformation
- Government escalating crackdown includes journalist accreditation revocations at NTV Uganda and Daily Monitor
- Uganda has history of election shutdowns: 4-day blackout in 2021, social media blocks in 2016
- Facebook remains blocked since 2021 elections; government previously blocked 100+ VPNs
- Pattern indicates high likelihood of digital restrictions during politically sensitive period

## Deep Dive

## Historical Context and Escalation Pattern

Uganda represents a textbook case of authoritarian digital control during elections. The government has systematically restricted internet access during every major election since 2011, with increasing sophistication. The 2021 election featured a comprehensive 4-day internet blackout plus targeted blocking of over 100 VPNs, demonstrating advanced technical capabilities for digital suppression. The fact that Facebook remains blocked nearly four years later indicates this isn't temporary election security but permanent platform control.

## Technical Infrastructure Under Threat

The UCC's directive to disable Starlink services is particularly significant for freedom tech builders. Satellite internet represents one of the few technologies capable of bypassing terrestrial infrastructure shutdowns, making this a direct countermeasure against censorship-resistant connectivity. The government's explicit threats against VPN users show they're actively working to close technical circumvention routes that activists and journalists rely on.

## Implications for Freedom Tech Deployment

Uganda's approach serves as a laboratory for digital authoritarianism that other governments study and replicate. The combination of infrastructure shutdowns, platform blocking, VPN suppression, and satellite internet disruption represents a multi-layered technical strategy. The government's willingness to disrupt mobile money systems (affecting millions of citizens economically) shows they'll accept significant collateral damage to maintain information control. This creates a hostile environment where mesh networking, offline-first applications, and hardware-based circumvention tools become essential rather than theoretical.

## What This Means for Builders

The Uganda case demonstrates why freedom tech must be designed for complete infrastructure hostility. Tools need to function without internet connectivity, resist VPN-style traffic analysis, and operate independently of both terrestrial ISPs and satellite providers under government pressure. The escalating technical sophistication of shutdowns - from simple social media blocks to comprehensive VPN suppression - shows builders are in an active arms race with state censorship capabilities.
