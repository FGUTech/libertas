# UK Court Orders Saudi Arabia to Pay £3m to Dissident Targeted with Pegasus Spyware

**TL;DR:** UK court awarded £3m to Saudi dissident Ghanem Al-Masarir after Citizen Lab proved his phones were infected with NSO Group's Pegasus spyware by Saudi operators, setting precedent for transnational surveillance accountability.

## Key Points

- UK court ordered Saudi Arabia to pay £3m to London-based dissident Ghanem Al-Masarir in January 2026
- Al-Masarir's iPhones were infected with NSO Group's Pegasus spyware by Saudi operator 'KINGDOM'
- Citizen Lab researcher Bill Marczak provided key forensic evidence proving spyware infection
- Damages awarded for psychological harm, lost earnings, and physical assault by Saudi agents
- Court judgment establishes legal precedent for accountability in transnational digital repression
- Case demonstrates sophisticated state-sponsored surveillance targeting dissidents abroad
- Citizen Lab initially discovered KINGDOM's targeting of Saudi dissidents in 2018
- Al-Masarir is a Saudi activist and political YouTuber operating from London

## Deep Dive

## Context and Legal Precedent

This ruling represents a watershed moment for accountability in transnational digital repression. The case originated from Citizen Lab's 2018 discovery of a Saudi operator called KINGDOM systematically targeting dissidents abroad with NSO Group's Pegasus spyware. The £3m award covers not just the digital surveillance but also associated physical harassment, demonstrating how digital and physical repression intersect in modern authoritarian tactics.

## Technical and Forensic Evidence

The case's strength relied heavily on Citizen Lab's technical forensics capabilities. Senior researcher Bill Marczak's testimony provided definitive proof that Al-Masarir's iPhones were infected after he clicked malicious links. The judge praised Marczak's "impeccable" qualifications and noted his evidence met the "requisite standard" for proving spyware infection and Saudi responsibility. This sets important precedent for the admissibility and weight of technical forensic evidence in surveillance cases.

## Implications for Freedom Tech and Activism

The ruling creates significant implications for both surveillance accountability and activist protection. As Citizen Lab director Ron Deibert noted, victims of targeted espionage have historically "lacked an avenue for justice" - this case provides that avenue through UK courts. For freedom tech builders, this demonstrates the critical importance of forensic capabilities in proving surveillance, while also highlighting how sophisticated nation-state surveillance tools are being weaponized against diaspora communities.

## What This Means for the Surveillance Economy

The financial penalty, while modest for a nation-state, establishes legal liability for customers of commercial spyware vendors like NSO Group. This could influence the risk calculus for both spyware vendors and their government clients, potentially making Western legal systems a meaningful enforcement mechanism against transnational digital repression.
