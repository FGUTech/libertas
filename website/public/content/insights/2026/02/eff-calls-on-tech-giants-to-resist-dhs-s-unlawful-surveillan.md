# EFF Calls on Tech Giants to Resist DHS's Unlawful Surveillance Subpoenas Targeting Activists

**TL;DR:** EFF and ACLU are demanding major tech companies require court orders before complying with DHS subpoenas that target First Amendment activities, after DHS withdrew challenged subpoenas rather than defend them in court.

## Key Points

- EFF sent open letter to 10 major tech companies (Meta, Google, Amazon, Apple, Discord, Microsoft, Reddit, SNAP, TikTok, X) demanding stronger resistance to DHS subpoenas
- DHS has systematically targeted users documenting ICE activities, criticizing government, and attending protests using administrative subpoenas
- When challenged in court, DHS withdrew subpoenas rather than defend their legality, proving government knows they're unlawful
- Administrative subpoenas don't require judicial approval, unlike search warrants - companies can legally refuse without court order
- Google complied with subpoena targeting Cornell PhD student who attended Gaza protest, breaking their own user notification promise
- DHS subpoenaed Meta to unmask anonymous Instagram accounts tracking ICE raids in California and Pennsylvania
- EFF demands: require court intervention, maximize user notification, resist gag orders that prevent user notification

## Deep Dive

## Legal Strategy and Precedent

This campaign represents a critical test of corporate resistance to government surveillance overreach. Administrative subpoenas are investigative tools that federal agencies can issue without judicial oversight, but companies are not legally compelled to comply without a court order. The fact that DHS consistently withdraws subpoenas when challenged reveals the agency's awareness of their legal weakness - a pattern that creates strong precedent for systematic corporate resistance.

## Technical and Operational Impact

The surveillance targets reveal DHS's focus on digital infrastructure for activism: anonymous Instagram accounts documenting ICE raids, Google accounts of protest attendees, and communications of government critics. This pattern demonstrates how administrative subpoenas have become a mass surveillance tool targeting constitutionally protected activities. Google received 28,622 subpoenas and Meta received 14,520 in just the first half of 2025, though these numbers aren't broken down by agency or type.

## Freedom Tech Implications

Corporate resistance to unlawful government data requests is a cornerstone of digital civil liberties infrastructure. When major platforms comply without judicial review, they effectively deputize themselves as surveillance arms of the state. The EFF's three-point framework - requiring court intervention, maximizing user notification, and resisting gag orders - provides a concrete model for how centralized platforms can protect users while remaining within legal boundaries.

## What This Means for the Ecosystem

This campaign could establish new industry standards for resisting government overreach, but it also highlights the structural vulnerability of centralized platforms. The pattern of targeting activists, protesters, and ICE critics shows how administrative subpoenas enable fishing expeditions against protected speech. Success here would strengthen the legal framework for corporate resistance, while failure could normalize compliance with legally questionable government surveillance.
