# DHS Using Administrative Subpoenas to Demand User Data from Tech Companies

**TL;DR:** Department of Homeland Security is abusing administrative subpoenas to force tech companies like Google to hand over names and locations of users who criticize government online.

## Key Points

- DHS demanding user names, locations, and personal data from tech companies using administrative subpoenas
- Targeting users who criticize government online according to EFF reporting
- Administrative subpoenas bypass normal court oversight processes unlike traditional warrants
- EFF providing guidance to tech companies on how to resist these requests
- Part of broader pattern including ICE facial recognition surveillance programs
- EFF campaigning for expanded end-to-end encryption protections in response
- Section 230 protections identified as critical defense for online speech rights

## Deep Dive

## Context and Background

The Department of Homeland Security has escalated its surveillance tactics by leveraging administrative subpoenas—a tool that bypasses traditional judicial oversight—to obtain personal information about users who engage in online criticism of government. This represents a significant expansion of domestic surveillance capabilities, targeting constitutionally protected speech activities. EFF Senior Staff Attorney F. Mario Trujillo specifically noted how these subpoenas differ from court orders in their lack of judicial review.

## Technical and Legal Implications

Administrative subpoenas require no probable cause or judicial approval, creating a low-barrier pathway for government agencies to access user data. Unlike traditional warrants or court orders, these tools operate with minimal oversight, making them attractive for fishing expeditions against government critics. The practice effectively creates a chilling effect on First Amendment-protected speech by demonstrating that online criticism can trigger government data collection.

## Threat Model Considerations

This development significantly alters the threat landscape for activists, journalists, and ordinary citizens engaging in government criticism online. The surveillance pipeline now extends from social media platforms directly to federal agencies without meaningful legal barriers. Users must assume that criticism of government policies could result in personal data collection, requiring enhanced operational security practices including use of pseudonyms, VPNs, and encrypted communications.

## Builder Implications

Tech companies face pressure to implement stronger user privacy protections by default, including data minimization practices and robust legal response procedures. The EFF's guidance to companies on resisting these subpoenas highlights the need for platforms to develop institutional capabilities to challenge government overreach. Developers should prioritize end-to-end encryption, anonymous account creation, and other technical measures that limit the data available for collection in the first place.
