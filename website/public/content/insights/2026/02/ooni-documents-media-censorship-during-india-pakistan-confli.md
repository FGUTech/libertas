# OONI Documents Media Censorship During India-Pakistan Conflict

**TL;DR:** OONI and Digital Rights Foundation documented coordinated media censorship by both India and Pakistan during their May 2025 conflict, with governments blocking news outlets citing 'disinformation' and 'hate speech.'

## Key Points

- 4-day India-Pakistan conflict in May 2025 triggered coordinated media censorship
- Both governments blocked multiple news media outlets during the armed conflict
- Censorship justified as fighting 'disinformation campaigns' and 'hate speech'
- OONI (Open Observatory of Network Interference) measured and documented the blocks
- Collaboration with Digital Rights Foundation Pakistan provided on-ground context
- Report provides technical evidence of information control tactics during crisis
- Demonstrates how governments use conflict as pretext for expanded censorship
- OONI's measurement infrastructure captured real-time censorship deployment

## Deep Dive

## Context and Escalation Pattern

The May 2025 India-Pakistan conflict followed a predictable escalation pattern: terrorist attack in Kashmir on April 22nd, rising political tensions, then a 4-day armed conflict involving drones and missiles [Source]. What's significant is how quickly both governments deployed coordinated information control measures, suggesting pre-existing capabilities and protocols for rapid media suppression during crises.

## Technical Measurement Infrastructure

OONI's ability to capture real-time censorship data during an active conflict demonstrates the critical value of distributed measurement infrastructure. Their collaboration with Digital Rights Foundation Pakistan likely provided crucial on-ground verification and context that pure technical measurements cannot capture. This partnership model—combining technical measurement with local digital rights expertise—appears to be essential for documenting censorship in high-tension environments.

## Information Control Tactics

Both governments using identical justifications ('disinformation campaigns' and 'hate speech') suggests either coordinated messaging or convergent evolution of censorship rhetoric. The speed of implementation indicates these weren't ad-hoc decisions but likely involved pre-planned block lists and rapid deployment mechanisms. This pattern of mutual censorship during bilateral conflicts creates information vacuums that both sides can exploit.

## Implications for Freedom Tech

This case highlights the need for censorship-resistant news distribution systems that can operate during information crackdowns. The coordinated nature of the blocking suggests that traditional circumvention tools may face simultaneous pressure from multiple state actors, requiring more robust and distributed approaches to information access.
