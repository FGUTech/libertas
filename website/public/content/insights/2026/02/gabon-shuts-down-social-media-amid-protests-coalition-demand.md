# Gabon Shuts Down Social Media Amid Protests, Coalition Demands Restoration

**TL;DR:** Gabon's government ordered indefinite social media shutdown during anti-government protests, blocking Facebook, WhatsApp, TikTok, Instagram and YouTube. 345+ human rights orgs demand immediate restoration.

## Key Points

- High Authority for Communication ordered indefinite social media suspension on February 17, 2026
- OONI confirms blocking of Facebook, WhatsApp, TikTok, Instagram, and YouTube as of February 18
- Shutdown coincides with ongoing anti-government protests and labor strikes
- #KeepItOn coalition of 345+ organizations from 105 countries demands immediate reversal
- Government claims measures prevent 'false information' spread and protect national unity
- Follows pattern: shutdowns in 2023 elections, 2021 COVID protests, 2019 coup attempt, 2016 election protests
- Coalition calls on ISPs (Moov Gabon, Gabon Telecom, Airtel Gabon) to resist shutdown orders
- Evidence shows shutdowns actually amplify misinformation rather than reduce it

## Deep Dive

## Context and Pattern

Gabon's latest shutdown represents a well-established authoritarian playbook, with documented incidents in 2023 (election period), 2021 (COVID protests), 2019 (coup attempt), and 2016 (election protests). The current shutdown under President Nguema's government follows the same pattern as the previous Bongo administration, suggesting institutional rather than leadership-driven censorship practices. The timing during anti-government protests and labor strikes indicates tactical deployment of communication restrictions to suppress organizing.

## Technical Infrastructure Implications

OONI's real-time confirmation of platform-specific blocking demonstrates sophisticated Deep Packet Inspection (DPI) capabilities rather than crude internet kill switches. The selective targeting of major social platforms while potentially maintaining basic internet suggests infrastructure capable of granular content filtering. This technical precision indicates state-level investment in censorship technology and potentially foreign assistance in implementation.

## Resistance Framework

The coalition's direct appeal to ISPs represents an evolution in shutdown resistance strategy, recognizing telecommunications companies as key chokepoints rather than passive intermediaries. By naming specific providers (Moov Gabon, Gabon Telecom, Airtel Gabon) and invoking UN Guiding Principles on Business and Human Rights, activists are attempting to create corporate liability for shutdown compliance. This approach could establish precedent for private sector pushback against government censorship orders.

## Freedom Tech Opportunities

The repeated shutdown pattern in Gabon creates clear demand for censorship-resistant communication tools. The focus on social media platforms suggests mesh networking solutions, decentralized messaging protocols, and circumvention tools could provide immediate value. The labor strike context particularly highlights need for coordination tools that function during network restrictions.
