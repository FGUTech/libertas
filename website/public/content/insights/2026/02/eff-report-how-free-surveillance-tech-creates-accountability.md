# EFF Report: How 'Free' Surveillance Tech Creates Accountability Gaps and Mass Data Sharing

**TL;DR:** EFF analysis reveals how surveillance vendors use 'free' trials, continued service without funding, and donations to bypass local oversight, creating data pipelines to ICE and eroding civil liberties.

## Key Points

- Surveillance vendors offer 'free' ALPRs, face recognition, drones, and data platforms to bypass local oversight processes
- Denver running competing drone trials from Flock Safety and Skydio/Axon for drone-as-first-responder programs through August 2026
- Companies continue providing services even after funding ends - Fall River used ShotSpotter for free after refusing $90k annual cost
- Denver mayor kept Flock ALPR cameras running despite city council unanimously rejecting $666k contract extension
- Data sharing agreements create pipelines to ICE, undermining sanctuary city protections
- Subscription models mean 'free' tools eventually require taxpayer funding for expanded feature sets
- Community groups launched grassroots campaigns demanding surveillance systems be shut off, not just defunded
- EFF recommends cities reject federal grants, vendor trials, and donations that facilitate surveillance experimentation

## Deep Dive

## Pattern of Surveillance Expansion

This EFF analysis documents a systematic approach by surveillance technology companies to circumvent democratic oversight through 'free' offerings. The pattern is clear: vendors provide initial access without cost to establish technical infrastructure and data collection, then leverage that deployment to secure ongoing contracts. The Denver case exemplifies this - after city council rejected Flock's ALPR contract, the mayor's office maintained the surveillance network under the guise of a 'task force review.'

## Technical and Legal Implications

The integration of multiple surveillance technologies creates concerning technical capabilities. Denver's drone trials combine aerial surveillance with ALPR technology, enabling persistent tracking from above. These systems generate detailed movement and association records that flow to federal agencies like ICE, effectively nullifying local sanctuary policies. The subscription model means initial 'free' deployments inevitably expand into comprehensive surveillance suites.

## Community Resistance Models

The grassroots response in Denver demonstrates effective resistance tactics. Rather than accepting the mayor's workaround, advocacy groups like the Denver Taskforce to Reimagine Policing and Transforming Our Communities Alliance launched direct campaigns demanding immediate shutdown. This approach recognizes that once surveillance infrastructure is deployed, the burden shifts to communities to actively remove it rather than simply block renewals.

## Strategic Considerations for Freedom Tech

This analysis reveals critical gaps where privacy-preserving alternatives could provide value. Communities seeking public safety solutions need tools that don't create mass surveillance infrastructure or federal data sharing pathways. The emphasis on local oversight mechanisms suggests appetite for transparency tools that could monitor government surveillance deployment and data sharing agreements.
