# Iran's Total Internet Shutdown Enables Mass State Violence During 2026 Uprising

**TL;DR:** Iran imposed a 15-day total internet blackout during nationwide protests, enabling unprecedented state violence with 30,000+ deaths while regime-aligned voices abroad filled the information vacuum to shape international narratives.

## Key Points

- Iran shut down internet completely from January 8-23, 2026 during nationwide protests across 30+ provinces
- NetBlocks confirmed near-complete connectivity collapse affecting mobile, fixed-line, and international gateways
- State crackdown resulted in over 30,000 deaths according to TIME citing anonymous officials, with 41,800-50,000 arrests reported by HRANA
- UN Human Rights Council described violence as 'unprecedented in its scope and brutality'
- Regime-aligned voices abroad, including NIAC's Trita Parsi, filled information vacuum during blackout
- Iranian state intellectuals explicitly discussed building 'Iran lobby' in US to manage diaspora narratives
- Systematic disinformation playbook emerged: manufacturing doubt, questioning evidence, blaming foreign manipulation
- Pattern mirrors November 2019 and Woman Life Freedom movement shutdowns
- Blackout enabled state violence while preventing real-time documentation and international awareness

## Deep Dive

## Context and Scale

Iran's 2026 internet shutdown represents the most extreme case yet documented of using connectivity blackouts to enable mass state violence. What began as strikes in Tehran's Grand Bazaar on December 27, 2025 rapidly expanded into nationwide demonstrations across more than 30 provinces. The government's response—a total internet shutdown from January 8-23, 2026—created a near-perfect information blackout that NetBlocks confirmed affected all connectivity channels: mobile networks, fixed-line services, and international gateways.

## Information Warfare During Digital Blackouts

The shutdown's most significant impact was creating an information vacuum that regime-aligned actors systematically exploited. Iranian state intellectuals Foad Izadi and Bijan Abdolkarimi explicitly discussed building an "Iran lobby" in the US, distinguishing it from an "Iranian lobby" to include non-Iranian voices. This reveals how authoritarian states conceptualize diaspora influence operations during domestic crises. The systematic disinformation playbook included manufacturing doubt about verified evidence, recasting political uprising as economic grievance, and attributing documented state violence to foreign agents.

## Technical and Strategic Implications

This case demonstrates how total connectivity shutdowns serve dual purposes: enabling state violence by preventing real-time documentation, and creating information voids that can be filled with regime narratives internationally. The 15-day duration allowed unprecedented violence to unfold largely unseen, with casualty figures only emerging after limited connectivity returned. For freedom tech builders, this highlights the critical need for communication tools that function during complete network shutdowns.

## Lessons for Resilient Communications

The Iran case shows that internet shutdowns are increasingly used not just to prevent coordination, but to create plausible deniability for mass violence. The regime's ability to control international narratives during the blackout—through aligned voices abroad and systematic disinformation—suggests that circumventing shutdowns requires both technical solutions and strategies for maintaining authentic information channels during connectivity collapse.
