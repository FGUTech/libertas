# MTN Enabled Gov't Bounty SMS System Linked to Extrajudicial Killings in Congo

**TL;DR:** Access Now demands MTN Group investigate how Congo's presidential security forces used MTN's network to send bounty SMS messages offering rewards for informants, amid reports of summary executions.

## Key Points

- Congo's DGSP (presidential security) sent SMS bounties via MTN Congo offering money for gang informants
- Campaign linked to crackdown beginning Sept 2024 that reportedly includes summary executions
- DGSP operates outside legal mandate - tasked with presidential security, not public order
- Access Now filed open letter demanding MTN Group investigate data breach and law enforcement practices
- Telco infrastructure weaponized for state surveillance enabling arbitrary detentions and disappearances
- MTN Group as majority shareholder of MTN Congo must review subscriber data sharing policies
- Case demonstrates how mobile networks can become surveillance tools for authoritarian governments
- Congo law specifically requires telcos to protect subscriber data - MTN allegedly violated duty of care

## Deep Dive

## Context and Background

This case represents a clear example of telecommunications infrastructure being co-opted for state surveillance and repression. President Denis Sassou N'Guess authorized the General Directorate of Presidential Security (DGSP) to conduct operations against alleged gangs in Brazzaville starting September 2024, despite this being outside their legal mandate of protecting presidential security. The campaign has reportedly escalated to extrajudicial killings, violating Congo's constitutional prohibition on capital punishment.

## Technical and Operational Concerns

The core issue is how DGSP gained access to MTN Congo's SMS infrastructure to broadcast bounty messages to subscribers. This suggests either: 1) MTN Congo provided direct API access to government forces, 2) Government forces gained unauthorized access to MTN systems, or 3) MTN facilitated the campaign through standard bulk SMS services without proper human rights due diligence. The lack of transparency around data sharing agreements between MTN Congo and law enforcement agencies creates systemic vulnerabilities for subscribers.

## Threat Model Implications

This incident demonstrates how mobile network operators can become unwitting (or willing) participants in state surveillance apparatus. The bounty SMS system transforms ordinary citizens into informants while creating a surveillance network that leverages social connections and financial incentives. For privacy advocates and activists, this highlights the critical importance of understanding telecom data sharing policies and the need for stronger legal protections around subscriber data.

## What Builders Should Consider

This case underscores why decentralized communication systems and mesh networks are essential infrastructure for regions with authoritarian governments. The incident also highlights the need for privacy-preserving reporting mechanisms that don't rely on traditional telecom infrastructure. Builders should consider how their tools might be misused by state actors and implement safeguards accordingly.
