# Iran's DPI-Powered Internet Whitelisting System Built with Russian Tech Support

**TL;DR:** Iran deployed infrastructure-level internet whitelisting using Deep Packet Inspection during 2025-26 protests, with Russian company Protei providing DPI systems to major Iranian telecoms, enabling granular traffic control beyond previous shutdowns.

## Key Points

- Iran imposed 15-day total internet shutdown (Jan 8-23, 2026) during protests with 5,000-20,000 casualties reported
- Authorities switched from service-level to infrastructure-level whitelisting using Deep Packet Inspection (DPI)
- Russian-origin company Protei supplied DPI systems to MTN Irancell and Ariantel, Iran's largest mobile providers
- New system enables blocking by traffic type (all messengers, video streaming) rather than just specific apps
- Protei operates from Estonia and Jordan, selling surveillance tech to telecoms across multiple authoritarian states
- DPI platform provides URL/domain blacklisting, DNS filtering, and scheduled blocking capabilities
- Citizen Lab previously documented Protei building mobile surveillance systems for Iranian authorities
- Technology represents evolution from Russia's own DPI deployment starting 2020 after Sandvine blacklisting

## Deep Dive

## Technical Architecture Evolution

Iran's 2026 internet shutdown represents a significant evolution in state censorship capabilities, moving from service-level restrictions to comprehensive infrastructure-level whitelisting. The system relies on Deep Packet Inspection deployed at network infrastructure points, allowing authorities to identify and block traffic by type rather than just destination. This enables blocking "all messengers" or "all video streaming" regardless of specific applications used.

## Russian Technology Transfer

The sophisticated censorship architecture traces back to Russian expertise developed after 2020, when Western sanctions forced Russia to develop domestic DPI capabilities. Protei, with roots in St. Petersburg but operating from Estonia and Jordan, has become a key vendor for authoritarian regimes seeking advanced censorship tools. The company's client list spans multiple regions with poor human rights records, suggesting systematic export of surveillance infrastructure.

## Implications for Circumvention

Infrastructure-level whitelisting poses unprecedented challenges for traditional circumvention tools. Unlike previous shutdowns that activists could bypass with VPNs or alternative networks, this system can identify and block VPN traffic patterns, Tor connections, and other circumvention protocols at the packet level. The scheduling capabilities mean authorities can dynamically adjust restrictions based on threat levels.

## Broader Surveillance Ecosystem

Protei's integration with major telecoms across Iran, Central Asia, and Africa indicates coordinated deployment of surveillance infrastructure. The company's ability to operate from EU jurisdiction (Estonia) while serving sanctioned regimes highlights enforcement gaps in technology export controls. This model may become template for other authoritarian governments seeking sophisticated internet control.
