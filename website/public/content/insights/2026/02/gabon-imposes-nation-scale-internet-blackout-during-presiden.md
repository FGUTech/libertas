# Gabon Imposes Nation-Scale Internet Blackout During Presidential Elections

**TL;DR:** Gabon cut internet access nationwide during August 2023 elections, citing violence prevention. Service was restored after military coup ended the president's rule, highlighting critical need for censorship-resistant communications.

## Key Points

- NetBlocks confirmed nation-scale internet blackout in Gabon on August 26, 2023 election day
- Government justified shutdown to "prevent spread of calls for violence" and "false information"
- Blackout lasted until August 30 when military officers seized power and placed President Ali Bongo under house arrest
- Communication Minister Rodrigue Mboumba Bissawou announced indefinite internet suspension via state TV
- Territory-wide curfew imposed from 7 PM to 6 AM starting August 27
- Pattern continues across West/Central Africa - similar shutdowns in Zambia, Congo, Uganda, Tanzania, Guinea, and others
- Previous internet cuts occurred during Gabon's 2019 attempted coup
- Demonstrates exact use case where mesh networks and satellite communications become essential

## Deep Dive

## Context and Pattern Recognition

Gabon's election-day internet shutdown represents a well-established authoritarian playbook across sub-Saharan Africa. President Ali Bongo's family had maintained power for 56 years, making this a high-stakes election where information control became paramount. The government's justification of preventing "calls for violence" and "false information" mirrors language used by other African governments to legitimize digital suppression.

## Technical and Strategic Implications

NetBlocks' real-time monitoring revealed the shutdown affected "multiple internet providers" simultaneously, indicating coordinated action at the infrastructure level rather than selective platform blocking. This suggests authorities either compelled ISPs to comply or controlled critical internet gateways. The fact that connectivity was restored immediately after the military coup demonstrates the shutdown was politically rather than technically motivated.

## Freedom Tech Relevance

This case perfectly illustrates why censorship-resistant communications infrastructure is critical for democratic processes. Traditional internet dependency creates single points of failure that authoritarian governments routinely exploit during sensitive periods. The timing - election day through vote counting - shows how information blackouts can undermine election transparency and opposition coordination.

## Regional Implications

The report's documentation of similar shutdowns across West and Central Africa (Zambia, Congo, Uganda, Tanzania, Guinea, Burundi, Togo, Mauritania, Benin, Cameroon, Mali) reveals a systematic regional trend. This pattern suggests coordinated learning between governments and highlights the urgent need for freedom tech deployment across the region, not just isolated countries.
