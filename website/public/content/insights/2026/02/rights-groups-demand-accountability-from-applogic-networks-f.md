# Rights Groups Demand Accountability from AppLogic Networks for Egypt Censorship Infrastructure

**TL;DR:** Human rights coalition demands transparency from AppLogic Networks (formerly Sandvine) over withdrawal from Egypt, where its deep packet inspection tech blocked 630+ websites including independent media outlets.

## Key Points

- 10 human rights orgs demand AppLogic Networks provide evidence of complete withdrawal from Egypt by Feb 26, 2026
- AppLogic's deep packet inspection technology used to block 630+ websites in Egypt, targeting independent media
- Company committed to exiting Egypt by Dec 31, 2025 as condition for removal from US Commerce Entity List
- Recent blocks continue: Brussels-based Zawia3 outlet blocked Feb 20, 2025, along with backup domain
- Technical research from Citizen Lab and Egyptian orgs like Masaar documented mass censorship traceable to AppLogic tech
- AppLogic cancelled engagement meetings with Egyptian civil society 'at last minute' citing vague safety concerns
- Coalition questions whether promised 1% profit donations for remediation reached affected Egyptian organizations
- Demands include evidence of hardware removal, plans for stakeholder engagement, and disclosure of remediation funds

## Deep Dive

## Background and Scale

AppLogic Networks (formerly Sandvine) represents a critical case study in how Western surveillance technology enables authoritarian censorship at scale. Technical research from Citizen Lab and Egyptian organizations like Masaar has documented systematic blocking of over 630 websites using AppLogic's deep packet inspection (DPI) technology, with independent media disproportionately targeted. The company's inclusion on the US Commerce Entity List forced public commitments to exit Egypt and other non-democratic jurisdictions by December 31, 2025.

## Technical and Operational Concerns

The ongoing blocking of outlets like Zawia3 in February 2025 raises critical questions about whether AppLogic equipment remains operational in Egypt despite claimed withdrawal. DPI infrastructure requires physical hardware presence and ongoing maintenance contracts, making complete disengagement technically complex. The coalition's demand for "detailed report on any remaining AppLogic-branded or related hardware" reflects understanding that announcing withdrawal differs from physically removing and disabling surveillance infrastructure.

## Accountability Gap

The pattern described—cancelled stakeholder meetings, lack of transparency about remediation funding, continued censorship despite claimed exit—highlights broader challenges in holding surveillance vendors accountable. AppLogic's commitment to donate 1% of profits for remediation appears to lack verification mechanisms, while promised civil society engagement has been repeatedly delayed or cancelled.

## Implications for Freedom Tech

This case demonstrates both the persistent harm from surveillance infrastructure and the potential for policy pressure to force vendor accountability. The Entity List designation created real leverage, but effective remediation requires sustained monitoring and verification mechanisms that current frameworks lack.
