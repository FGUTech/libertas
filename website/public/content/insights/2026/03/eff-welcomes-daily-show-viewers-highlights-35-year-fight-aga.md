# EFF Welcomes Daily Show Viewers, Highlights 35-Year Fight Against Digital Surveillance

**TL;DR:** The Electronic Frontier Foundation showcases its three-decade battle against government surveillance, from the Crypto Wars to NSA dragnet spying, while promoting Executive Director Cindy Cohn's new book.

## Key Points

- EFF promotes its mission to Daily Show audience, highlighting 35 years defending digital civil liberties
- Features Cindy Cohn's book 'Privacy's Defender' covering Crypto Wars, NSA spying revelations, and FBI gag order fights
- Founded in 1990 after Secret Service raids on Steve Jackson Games, establishing email privacy precedents
- Co-founded by Mitch Kapor (Lotus), John Perry Barlow (Grateful Dead), John Gilmore (Sun Microsystems), and Steve Wozniak (Apple)
- Won landmark Bernstein v. DOJ case establishing code as protected speech during encryption export restrictions
- Advocates 'Privacy First' approach to address online harms by targeting corporate surveillance infrastructure
- Organization supported by 30,000+ members, positioning itself as 'infrastructure of rebellion'
- Early case established that electronic mail deserves same protection as telephone calls

## Deep Dive

## Historical Context

EFF's origin story represents a pivotal moment in digital rights history. The 1990 Secret Service raids on Steve Jackson Games revealed how poorly existing civil liberties organizations understood emerging digital technologies. The seizure of computers and reading of customer emails without charges being filed demonstrated the urgent need for technologically-informed legal advocacy. The case's resolution - establishing that email deserves telephone-level protection - created foundational precedent for digital communications privacy.

## Technical and Legal Precedents

The Bernstein v. DOJ case mentioned represents a crucial victory in the Crypto Wars, where the government attempted to classify encryption software as weapons subject to export controls. This case established source code as protected speech under the First Amendment, enabling the widespread deployment of strong cryptography that underpins modern secure communications. EFF's 'Privacy First' framework directly addresses the surveillance capitalism model by targeting the data collection infrastructure that enables multiple forms of online harm.

## Strategic Implications

EFF's positioning as the 'infrastructure of rebellion' reflects their understanding that digital rights underpin all other activist movements. Their focus on anticipating 'the next big thing in tech' - from age verification to AI to surveillance companies like Palantir - demonstrates proactive rather than reactive advocacy. The organization's emphasis on corporate surveillance as the root cause of internet harms provides a systemic approach that freedom tech builders should consider when designing privacy-preserving alternatives.

## What This Means for Builders

EFF's sustained success over three decades provides a model for long-term institutional thinking in freedom tech. Their combination of legal expertise, technical understanding, and public advocacy creates a template for defending emerging technologies against government overreach. The Privacy First framework offers builders a policy roadmap aligned with technical solutions that reduce surveillance rather than simply regulating its use.
