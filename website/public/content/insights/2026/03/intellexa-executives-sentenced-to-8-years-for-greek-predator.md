# Intellexa Executives Sentenced to 8 Years for Greek Predator Spyware Scandal

**TL;DR:** Four Intellexa executives including the founder received 8-year prison sentences for deploying Predator spyware against 90+ Greek public figures - the first criminal conviction of mercenary spyware company leadership.

## Key Points

- Greek court sentenced 4 Intellexa executives to 8 years prison for 2022 'Predatorgate' scandal
- Predator spyware targeted over 90 Greek politicians, journalists, and public figures
- Citizen Lab confirmed infections on phones of journalist Thanasis Koukakis and former Meta employee Artemis Seaford
- First-ever criminal conviction and prison sentence for mercenary spyware company executives
- Convictions expected to significantly hamper Intellexa's future business operations
- Victory for investigative journalists and civil society organizations tracking commercial spyware
- Sets legal precedent for holding spyware vendors criminally accountable

## Deep Dive

## Historic Legal Precedent

This conviction represents a watershed moment in the fight against commercial spyware. While governments have imposed sanctions and export controls on spyware vendors, criminal prosecution of company executives has remained elusive. The 8-year sentences send a clear signal that deploying surveillance technology against civil society carries real legal risk.

## Technical and Operational Impact

Predator spyware, developed by Intellexa, is a sophisticated mobile surveillance tool capable of extracting messages, calls, location data, and activating device cameras/microphones. The Greek operation's scale - targeting over 90 individuals including politicians and journalists - demonstrates the industrial scale of modern mercenary surveillance. Citizen Lab's technical analysis provided crucial forensic evidence linking infections to specific infrastructure.

## Implications for the Spyware Industry

As Citizen Lab's Scott-Railton noted, these convictions create a 'huge ball and chain' for Intellexa's operations. The precedent may deter other spyware vendors or force them to implement stricter controls over client usage. However, the industry's international nature means enforcement remains challenging - many vendors operate across jurisdictions to minimize legal exposure.

## What This Means for Defenders

The convictions validate the critical role of technical research organizations like Citizen Lab and investigative journalism in exposing surveillance abuses. The success relied on forensic mobile analysis, infrastructure mapping, and sustained public pressure - a model that could be replicated against other vendors.
