# Access Now: 2025 Saw Record Internet Shutdowns, 30 in 15 African Countries

**TL;DR:** Access Now's #KeepItOn coalition documented 313 internet shutdowns globally in 2025, including 30 shutdowns across 15 African countries, with protests and elections as primary triggers.

## Key Points

- 313 internet shutdowns recorded globally in 52 countries during 2025 - highest number ever documented
- Africa experienced 30 shutdowns across 15 countries, with not a single day in 2025 passing without at least one global shutdown
- Angola shut down internet for first time in July 2025 for 3+ hours during anti-government fuel price protests
- Protests were leading trigger (14 shutdowns in 8 countries), followed by election-related shutdowns (8 shutdowns in 4 countries)
- Tanzania led with 8 shutdowns including twice during elections; DRC cut internet in Goma during humanitarian crisis
- Uganda continued blocking Facebook for fifth consecutive year; Equatorial Guinea maintained isolation of Annobón island
- Civil society demonstrated resilience through litigation and alternative documentation methods during shutdowns
- African Commission on Human and Peoples Rights raised concerns about shutdown impacts on democratic processes

## Deep Dive

## Context and Scale

The Access Now #KeepItOn coalition's 2025 report reveals an unprecedented escalation in internet shutdowns globally, with Africa accounting for nearly 10% of documented incidents. The fact that no single day passed without a shutdown somewhere in the world indicates systematic adoption of internet disruption as a governance tool. Angola's entry as a "new offender" suggests the tactic is spreading to previously unaffected regions, while entrenched patterns in countries like Uganda (5-year Facebook block) and Tanzania (8 shutdowns in one year) show institutionalization of digital repression.

## Technical and Strategic Implications

The protest-triggered shutdowns (14 incidents across 8 African countries) and election-related disruptions (8 incidents across 4 countries) reveal predictable patterns that freedom tech builders can anticipate. The Democratic Republic of Congo's shutdown of Goma "throughout a humanitarian crisis" demonstrates how authorities weaponize connectivity during vulnerable periods when communication is most critical. These patterns create clear use cases for mesh networking, satellite communications, and other censorship-resistant infrastructure.

## Resistance and Adaptation

The report's emphasis on "demonstrable resilience from civil society" including litigation against unconstitutional blocks and alternative documentation methods suggests growing sophistication in counter-shutdown tactics. However, the continued effectiveness of shutdowns in disrupting democratic processes indicates current workarounds remain insufficient for maintaining essential communications during critical periods.

## Builder Considerations

The geographic concentration in specific regions (15 African countries) combined with predictable triggers (protests, elections) creates opportunity for targeted deployment of freedom tech solutions. The African Commission on Human and Peoples Rights' recognition of shutdown impacts during elections provides potential institutional support for alternative communication infrastructure, though builders must balance visibility with operational security given the deliberate nature of these restrictions.
