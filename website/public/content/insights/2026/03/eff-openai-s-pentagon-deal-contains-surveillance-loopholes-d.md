# EFF: OpenAI's Pentagon Deal Contains Surveillance Loopholes Despite Public Assurances

**TL;DR:** EFF analysis reveals OpenAI's amended Pentagon contract uses "weasel words" like "intentionally" and "unconstrained" that could enable mass surveillance of US persons despite public promises.

## Key Points

- OpenAI faced criticism for Pentagon deal after Anthropic refused to drop surveillance restrictions
- ChatGPT uninstalls surged 295% following announcement of DoD partnership
- CEO Sam Altman amended contract to prohibit "intentional" domestic surveillance of US persons
- EFF identifies loopholes: "intentional," "deliberate," and "unconstrained" allow incidental collection
- Government historically uses "incidental" collection to justify mass surveillance programs
- Contract permits commercially acquired personal data and "constrained" monitoring
- NSA supposedly excluded but through secret agreements with technical assurances only
- EFF warns contract language provides public cover while retaining surveillance flexibility

## Deep Dive

## Government's Surveillance Interpretation Problem

The core issue EFF identifies is the fundamental disconnect between what the public understands as "no surveillance" versus how the US government interprets "applicable laws." The government has historically embraced expansive interpretations of surveillance authorities while fighting court oversight. OpenAI's contract language of being "consistent with applicable laws" essentially delegates surveillance limits to the same agencies that have repeatedly pushed legal boundaries.

## Technical and Legal Loopholes

The amended contract contains several critical ambiguities. "Intentionally" excludes incidental collection—the same loophole intelligence agencies use to justify sweeping up US person communications in ostensibly foreign-targeted programs. "Unconstrained monitoring" implies some level of constrained monitoring remains permissible. The prohibition on "deliberate tracking" still allows commercially purchased data and algorithmic inference patterns that don't constitute direct tracking.

## Precedent and Power Concentration

This follows a pattern where the Pentagon reportedly agreed to respect Anthropic's restrictions only "as appropriate," showing how government negotiators systematically preserve operational flexibility while offering public assurances. EFF emphasizes that relying on corporate executives and Pentagon officials to define civil liberties protections concentrates too much power in too few hands, especially when those entities have financial incentives to interpret restrictions narrowly.

## Implications for the AI Surveillance State

OpenAI's capitulation after Anthropic's refusal creates a competitive dynamic where surveillance-friendly AI companies gain government contracts while principled companies lose market opportunities. This economic pressure could systematically favor AI development that enables rather than restricts government surveillance capabilities, accelerating the technical infrastructure for mass monitoring.
