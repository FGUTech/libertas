# Congress Plans Clean FISA 702 Extension Despite Mass Surveillance Abuses

**TL;DR:** Congress is moving toward an 18-month clean extension of FISA Section 702 mass surveillance powers without warrant requirements or other reforms, despite documented abuses against activists, journalists, and lawmakers.

## Key Points

- House Speaker Mike Johnson confirms plan for clean FISA Section 702 extension for at least 18 months
- House Judiciary Chair Jim Jordan now supports clean extension, abandoning previous reform positions
- 2024 RISAA reauthorization expanded surveillance powers while adding only nominal reforms
- Section 702 previously abused to spy on protesters, lawmakers, journalists, campaign donors, and judges
- Three reform bills (SAFE, PLEWSA, GSRA) available but Congress not considering them
- No warrant requirement being implemented despite House previously voting for it
- Clean extension means continued warrantless collection and querying of Americans' communications
- EFF warns mass surveillance program operates without adequate safeguards or oversight

## Deep Dive

## Context and Background

Section 702 of FISA authorizes the NSA to conduct warrantless mass surveillance of foreign targets, but inevitably sweeps up massive amounts of Americans' communications. The 2024 RISAA reauthorization was supposed to be a temporary two-year extension to allow time for meaningful reforms, but Congress has failed to pursue them. Instead, leadership is now pushing for another extension without any additional protections.

## Pattern of Abuse and Expansion

The documented abuses are extensive and systematic. Prior to RISAA, Section 702 was misused to run improper queries targeting peaceful protesters, federal and state lawmakers, Congressional staff, thousands of campaign donors, journalists, and even a judge reporting civil rights violations. Rather than constraining these abuses, RISAA actually expanded the program by compelling a much larger group of people and service providers to assist with surveillance operations.

## Technical and Legal Implications

The "clean extension" approach means the government retains its ability to collect Americans' communications without warrants and query that data without judicial oversight. This represents a continuation of the "collect first, sort out targets second" approach that fundamentally inverts Fourth Amendment protections. The failure to implement warrant requirements means intelligence agencies can continue accessing Americans' private communications through backdoor searches of the 702 database.

## What This Means for Privacy Rights

The congressional abandonment of reform efforts, particularly Jim Jordan's reversal on warrant requirements, signals a significant retreat from privacy protections. With three viable reform bills available (SAFE, PLEWSA, GSRA), the choice of a clean extension represents a deliberate decision to maintain the surveillance status quo despite documented constitutional violations and abuse patterns.
