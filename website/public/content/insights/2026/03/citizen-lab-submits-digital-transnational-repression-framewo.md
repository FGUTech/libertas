# Citizen Lab Submits Digital Transnational Repression Framework to UN

**TL;DR:** Citizen Lab submitted recommendations to the UN on digital transnational repression - governments using digital tools to surveil and silence human rights defenders across borders.

## Key Points

- Citizen Lab submitted formal input to UN High Commissioner on Human Rights on digital transnational repression
- Digital transnational repression defined as governments using digital tools to surveil, intimidate, and silence diaspora activists
- Submission calls for UN to identify, document, and characterize these acts as human rights violations
- Recommendations target three actors: UN bodies, host countries, and technology companies
- Built on extensive Citizen Lab research documenting repression methods and victim impacts
- Aims to establish formal UN framework for addressing cross-border digital surveillance
- Could influence international policy on state surveillance of diaspora communities

## Deep Dive

## Context and Significance

This submission represents a critical step toward establishing international legal frameworks around digital transnational repression. Citizen Lab, with their extensive research documenting state surveillance campaigns against dissidents, is positioning their findings within UN human rights mechanisms. The timing aligns with growing awareness of how authoritarian governments are extending their reach digitally across borders to monitor and intimidate diaspora communities.

## Technical and Policy Implications

The submission's three-pronged approach targeting UN bodies, host countries, and technology companies suggests a comprehensive strategy for addressing this threat. For UN bodies, this could lead to formal recognition of digital transnational repression as a distinct category of human rights violation. Host countries may face pressure to implement stronger protections for diaspora communities, potentially including technical measures and legal frameworks. Technology companies could see increased scrutiny of their role in enabling state surveillance.

## What This Means for Freedom Tech

Formal UN recognition of digital transnational repression could validate the threat model that drives much freedom tech development. This framework may influence funding priorities, technical standards, and operational security practices for tools protecting at-risk users. The submission's emphasis on documenting methods and impacts also provides valuable intelligence for builders designing countermeasures.

## Implications for Builders

Builders should monitor how this submission influences UN policy development and whether it leads to specific technical recommendations. The framework may also provide legitimacy for privacy-preserving technologies when engaging with policymakers or securing funding. Understanding the documented methods of digital transnational repression can inform threat modeling for diaspora-focused tools.
