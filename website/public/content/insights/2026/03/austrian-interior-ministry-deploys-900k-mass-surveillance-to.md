# Austrian Interior Ministry Deploys €900K Mass Surveillance Tool Used by ICE

**TL;DR:** Austria's Interior Ministry spent €900K on Cobwebs' Tangles surveillance software that harvests social media and web data, with officials refusing to disclose usage details or capabilities.

## Key Points

- Austrian Interior Ministry purchased Tangles surveillance software for €900K from Israeli firm Cobwebs
- Tool gathers and analyzes data across social media platforms and web sources for mass surveillance
- Same software suite used by U.S. Immigration and Customs Enforcement (ICE)
- Ministry refuses to disclose what the tool will be used for or its surveillance scope
- Unclear if Austria also purchased WebLoc feature that tracks mobile device movements in designated areas
- Citizen Lab researchers discovered the purchase, raising concerns about democratic oversight
- Tool potentially enables surveillance using behavioral data from digital advertising networks
- Represents deployment of military-grade surveillance tech by democratic government without transparency

## Deep Dive

## Context and Significance

The Austrian Interior Ministry's acquisition of Cobwebs' Tangles represents a concerning trend of democratic governments deploying sophisticated surveillance infrastructure with minimal oversight. The €900K investment demonstrates significant commitment to mass data collection capabilities, while the ministry's refusal to provide details about usage parameters suggests awareness of the tool's controversial nature. The connection to ICE operations is particularly notable given that agency's controversial surveillance practices.

## Technical Capabilities and Threat Model

Tangles operates as a comprehensive social media and web intelligence platform, capable of harvesting data across multiple platforms simultaneously. The potential WebLoc addition would provide geolocation tracking of mobile devices within specified geographic boundaries, creating a powerful combination of digital and physical surveillance capabilities. The integration with digital advertising behavioral data represents a particularly invasive vector, potentially allowing authorities to profile citizens based on commercial tracking infrastructure.

## Democratic Governance Implications

The lack of transparency around deployment parameters creates a significant accountability gap in democratic oversight. Without clear usage guidelines, legal frameworks, or public disclosure of capabilities, such tools risk enabling unchecked surveillance operations that could chill free expression and association. The Austrian case may serve as a model for other EU governments seeking to deploy similar capabilities while maintaining plausible deniability about scope and targets.

## What This Means for Privacy and Freedom Tech

This deployment highlights the urgent need for technical countermeasures against commercial surveillance infrastructure that can be repurposed by state actors. The convergence of social media intelligence, behavioral advertising data, and geolocation tracking creates a comprehensive surveillance apparatus that traditional privacy tools may not adequately address. Builders should consider how existing privacy technologies perform against this type of multi-vector intelligence gathering.
