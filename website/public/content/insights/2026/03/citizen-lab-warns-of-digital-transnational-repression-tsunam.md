# Citizen Lab Warns of Digital Transnational Repression 'Tsunami' Facing Canadians

**TL;DR:** Citizen Lab director testified to Parliament that digital transnational repression will intensify due to U.S. authoritarianism, AI expansion, and Canada's failure to regulate mercenary spyware as promised.

## Key Points

- Ron Deibert testified to House of Commons subcommittee on March 23 about transnational repression threats
- Digital transnational repression uses surveillance tech to harass and intimidate victims across borders
- Creates 'major chilling effects' through paranoia, isolation, and self-censorship in civil society
- Three factors accelerating threat: U.S. authoritarianism, rapid AI spread, Canada's realist foreign policy shift
- Canada pledged to regulate mercenary spyware industry but has failed to follow through
- Recommends deep engagement with affected diaspora communities to tailor responses
- Calls for review of Canada-US Safe Third Country Agreement that blocks refugee claims

## Deep Dive

## Context and Significance

This testimony represents a rare high-level government briefing on the expanding threat of digital surveillance tools being weaponized across borders. Citizen Lab, the premier digital rights research organization that exposed NSO Group's Pegasus spyware operations, is warning Parliament that current trends point toward a dramatic escalation in cross-border digital repression targeting Canadian residents and diaspora communities.

## Technical and Policy Implications

The testimony highlights a critical gap between government promises and action on mercenary spyware regulation. Despite public commitments to address the industry that sells surveillance capabilities to authoritarian regimes, Canada has failed to implement meaningful controls. This regulatory vacuum occurs as AI capabilities rapidly expand surveillance possibilities and as geopolitical tensions increase incentives for states to target dissidents abroad.

## What This Means for Freedom Tech

Deibert's warning signals that traditional diplomatic and legal protections are failing to keep pace with digital surveillance capabilities. The identification of U.S. authoritarianism as a contributing factor suggests that even traditional democratic allies may become sources of transnational repression. This creates an urgent need for technical countermeasures and privacy tools that can protect vulnerable communities regardless of their host country's political trajectory.

## Strategic Considerations

The emphasis on diaspora community engagement suggests that effective responses require understanding specific threat models faced by different groups. The failure of existing regulatory approaches indicates that technical solutions and community-driven security practices may be more reliable than policy protections in the near term.
