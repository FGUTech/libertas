# Greek Court Convicts Spyware Executives in Landmark Predatorgate Ruling

**TL;DR:** Athens court found four Intellexa spyware executives guilty of unlawfully targeting journalists and politicians with Predator spyware, marking the first criminal accountability for surveillance tech companies in Greece.

## Key Points

- Athens court convicted four Intellexa executives for unlawful surveillance using Predator spyware
- Defendants include Tal Dilian, Sara Hamou, Felix Bitzios, and Giannis Lavranos - sentenced to 8 years each (suspended pending appeal)
- First criminal convictions in Greece's 'Predatorgate' scandal targeting journalists, politicians, and activists
- Court referred case back to prosecutors for felony-level investigation including potential espionage charges
- Predator spyware was used alongside traditional wiretaps by Greece's intelligence service against dozens of targets
- Ruling establishes legal precedent for holding surveillance technology companies criminally liable for abuse
- State officials who ordered the surveillance remain unprosecuted despite documented evidence of involvement
- Human Rights Watch documented chilling effects on media freedom and journalist intimidation from the scandal

## Deep Dive

## Context and Background

The Predatorgate scandal emerged in 2022 when journalists revealed that Greece's intelligence services had systematically targeted dozens of individuals—including journalists, politicians, and businesspeople—using Predator spyware developed by Greek company Intellexa. This surveillance campaign was conducted alongside traditional wiretapping operations, representing a comprehensive digital surveillance apparatus. The scandal drew international attention and a European Parliament investigation, which documented state involvement in the surveillance operations.

## Technical and Legal Significance

This ruling establishes crucial legal precedent by holding surveillance technology developers criminally liable for how their tools are deployed. The charges—unlawfully accessing information systems, violating communications privacy, and interfering with personal data systems—create a framework for prosecuting spyware companies whose products facilitate human rights abuses. The court's decision to refer the case for felony-level investigation, including potential espionage charges, suggests more serious consequences may follow.

## Threat Model Implications

The conviction demonstrates that criminal liability can extend beyond direct surveillance operators to technology vendors themselves. This creates new risk calculations for commercial spyware companies and may influence due diligence practices. However, the failure to prosecute state officials who ordered the surveillance reveals ongoing impunity for government actors, limiting the deterrent effect.

## What Builders Should Consider

This case illustrates both progress and limitations in surveillance accountability. While technology companies now face real criminal risk for enabling abuse, state actors remain largely protected. The documented chilling effects on journalism underscore the need for robust technical countermeasures against commercial spyware. Builders should note that legal accountability mechanisms are emerging but remain incomplete, emphasizing the importance of technical solutions that resist abuse by design.
