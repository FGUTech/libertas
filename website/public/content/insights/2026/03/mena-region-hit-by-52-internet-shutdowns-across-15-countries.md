# MENA Region Hit by 52 Internet Shutdowns Across 15 Countries in 2025

**TL;DR:** Access Now reports 313 global internet shutdowns in 2025, with MENA experiencing 52 shutdowns across 15 countries. Authorities increasingly target satellite internet alternatives.

## Key Points

- At least 313 internet shutdowns occurred globally in 52 countries during 2025, with not a single day without at least one shutdown
- MENA region experienced 52 shutdowns across 15 countries, with Iran leading at 11 shutdowns
- Armed conflict drives shutdowns in Palestine (7), Sudan (3), Syria (3), and Yemen (3), with Israel imposing all Palestinian shutdowns targeting Gaza
- Educational disruption systematic: Iraq imposed 6 exam-related shutdowns, while Algeria and Jordan blocked messaging apps during exams
- Authorities increasingly target Low Earth Orbit (LEO) satellite internet services in Iran, Syria, and Yemen as alternatives emerge
- Syria maintained Assad-era shutdown tactics despite regime change, demonstrating institutional continuity of digital repression
- Access Now calls for investment in 'secure, reliable, and scalable solutions that enable people to bypass shutdowns and censorship'

## Deep Dive

## Context and Escalation

The 2025 data represents an unprecedented normalization of internet shutdowns as a tool of state control, with the 313 global shutdowns marking the highest number ever recorded by the #KeepItOn coalition. The MENA region's 52 shutdowns across 15 countries demonstrate how digital repression has become institutionalized across diverse political contexts - from active war zones to stable authoritarian regimes conducting routine exam censorship.

## Technical Evolution of Shutdown Tactics

A critical development is authorities' pivot toward disrupting LEO satellite internet services in Iran, Syria, and Yemen. This represents a significant escalation in technical sophistication, as governments move beyond traditional ISP-level controls to target mesh and satellite-based alternatives that freedom tech communities have promoted as censorship-resistant infrastructure. The targeting of these "alternatives" suggests state actors are actively adapting to circumvention technologies.

## Implications for Freedom Tech Builders

The systematic nature of these shutdowns - including educational disruption as routine policy and the use of connectivity cuts as weapons of war - creates clear requirements for next-generation censorship resistance tools. The report's explicit call for "scalable solutions that enable people to bypass shutdowns" from a major digital rights organization signals institutional demand for mesh networking, satellite communication tools, and offline-capable applications that can maintain functionality during total connectivity blackouts.

## Threat Model Evolution

The geographical spread and technical targeting patterns reveal that internet shutdowns have evolved from emergency measures to routine governance tools. The fact that Syria maintained Assad-era shutdown tactics despite regime change demonstrates how shutdown infrastructure becomes embedded in state capacity regardless of political transitions, suggesting freedom tech solutions must be designed to resist institutionalized rather than merely opportunistic censorship.
