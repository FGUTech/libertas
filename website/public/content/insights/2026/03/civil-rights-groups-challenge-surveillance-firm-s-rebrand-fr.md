# Civil Rights Groups Challenge Surveillance Firm's Rebrand from Sandvine to AppLogic Networks

**TL;DR:** Access Now and other rights groups demand transparency from AppLogic (formerly Sandvine) after its removal from US Entity List, citing lack of remedy for DPI surveillance abuses in Egypt.

## Key Points

- AppLogic Networks (rebranded from Sandvine) removed from US Commerce Entity List in October 2024 after announcing corporate reforms
- Company was blacklisted in February 2024 for deep packet inspection (DPI) technology misuse by Egyptian authorities
- DPI systems enabled mass web monitoring, censorship, and spyware injection against human rights defenders and dissidents
- AppLogic committed to exiting Egypt by December 31, 2025, but has not provided transparent accounting of withdrawal
- Company has failed to engage with Egyptian civil society or provide remedies for past surveillance abuses
- Access Now and other rights groups warn that corporate reforms remain at level of public statements only
- Case exposes weaknesses in accountability mechanisms that rely on PR statements rather than verifiable actions

## Deep Dive

## Corporate Accountability Under Fire

The AppLogic Networks case represents a critical test of how accountability mechanisms handle surveillance technology companies that enable human rights abuses. Despite being removed from the US Entity List based on promised reforms, the company formerly known as Sandvine has failed to provide concrete evidence of meaningful change in its operations or remediation for documented harms.

## Technical and Operational Concerns

The core issue involves deep packet inspection technology that Egyptian authorities used for mass surveillance infrastructure. DPI systems can analyze network traffic in real-time, enabling content filtering, user tracking, and in this case, the injection of spyware targeting government critics. AppLogic's equipment potentially remains operational in Egypt despite withdrawal commitments, raising questions about ongoing surveillance capabilities and the company's technical responsibility for legacy deployments.

## Implications for Freedom Tech

This case highlights the inadequacy of current corporate accountability frameworks for surveillance technology vendors. The US government's decision to delist AppLogic based primarily on announced policy changes, rather than verifiable operational reforms, sets a concerning precedent. For freedom tech builders, this demonstrates the need for technical solutions that assume surveillance infrastructure will persist regardless of corporate policy statements.

## What This Means for the Ecosystem

The pattern of surveillance companies using rebranding and policy announcements to escape accountability without substantive change threatens the effectiveness of export controls and sanctions as human rights protection mechanisms. This creates an environment where surveillance vendors can continue operations with minimal real consequences for enabling repression.
