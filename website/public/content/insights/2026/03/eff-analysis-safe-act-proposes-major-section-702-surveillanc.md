# EFF Analysis: SAFE Act Proposes Major Section 702 Surveillance Reforms

**TL;DR:** The bipartisan SAFE Act would require FBI warrants for accessing Americans' communications collected via Section 702, address parallel construction, and partially close data broker loopholes.

## Key Points

- Senators Lee (R-UT) and Durbin (D-IL) introduced SAFE Act to reform Section 702 FISA surveillance authority
- Bill would require FBI warrants before accessing content of Americans' incidentally collected communications
- Would mandate disclosure of Section 702 evidence in court proceedings to prevent parallel construction
- Includes provisions to close data broker loophole for intelligence agencies purchasing location/personal data
- Section 702 expires April 2026, creating window for reform vs White House push for clean reauthorization
- FBI conducted 3.4 million warrantless searches of US persons' 702 data in 2021 alone
- EFF calls reforms imperfect but significant improvement over unconstitutional status quo
- Federal court recently ruled backdoor searches of 702 data unconstitutional

## Deep Dive

## Background and Stakes

Section 702 of FISA was designed for foreign surveillance but has become a routine domestic surveillance tool. The FBI's 3.4 million warrantless searches of US persons' data in 2021 demonstrates how "incidental collection" has become systematic domestic spying. With the authority expiring in April 2026 and the White House pushing for clean reauthorization, this represents a critical reform opportunity.

## Technical and Legal Implications

The SAFE Act addresses three core surveillance state vulnerabilities: warrantless backdoor searches, parallel construction to hide surveillance sources, and the data broker economy that lets agencies buy data they'd need warrants to collect directly. However, the warrant requirement only applies to content access - agencies can still query to see what data exists on a person before seeking judicial approval.

## What Builders Should Consider

This legislative fight will determine whether the US surveillance apparatus continues expanding or faces meaningful constraints. The data broker provisions are particularly significant - they would partially implement protections that individual states like Montana are pioneering. Privacy-focused builders should monitor how these debates shape the regulatory environment for data collection and sharing practices.
