# EFF Urges Supreme Court to Hold Cisco Liable for Torture-Enabling Surveillance System

**TL;DR:** EFF filed Supreme Court brief arguing Cisco should face liability for custom-building China's 'Golden Shield' surveillance system used to persecute, torture Falun Gong practitioners.

## Key Points

- EFF filed amicus brief in Cisco Systems v. Doe I case before U.S. Supreme Court
- Cisco custom-built 'Golden Shield' surveillance system for Chinese government targeting Falun Gong
- System enabled tracking, detention, and torture - intercepted communications used during torture sessions
- 9th Circuit ruled U.S. companies can be liable under Alien Tort Statute for aiding human rights abuses
- Court held companies need only 'knowledge' not 'purpose' that assistance enables abuse
- Supreme Court oral arguments scheduled for April 28, 2025
- Case could set precedent for accountability of surveillance tech companies globally
- EFF argues this goes beyond general-purpose tech to purposeful persecution assistance

## Deep Dive

## Legal Precedent at Stake

This Supreme Court case represents a critical juncture for tech company accountability in the surveillance economy. The 9th Circuit's 2023 ruling established that U.S. corporations can face liability under the Alien Tort Statute (ATS) for actions taken domestically that aid human rights abuses abroad. Crucially, the court ruled that companies need only have 'knowledge' rather than explicit 'purpose' that their assistance enables abuse - a lower bar that could impact how surveillance companies operate globally.

## Technical and Operational Context

Cisco's 'Golden Shield' system was not off-the-shelf networking equipment but a custom surveillance infrastructure designed specifically for the Chinese government's persecution apparatus. The system enabled comprehensive tracking and monitoring of Falun Gong practitioners, with intercepted communications reportedly used during torture sessions to force religious renunciation. This level of customization and integration with persecution mechanisms distinguishes the case from general technology sales.

## Implications for Freedom Tech Ecosystem

The Supreme Court's decision will establish whether U.S. courts remain accessible venues for challenging corporate complicity in digital authoritarianism. A ruling favoring Cisco could effectively shield surveillance companies from accountability, potentially accelerating the global proliferation of repressive monitoring systems. Conversely, upholding the 9th Circuit decision would create legal risk for companies building custom surveillance tools for authoritarian clients.

## Broader Surveillance Industry Impact

As EFF notes, Cisco represents just one example of U.S. companies providing surveillance capabilities to repressive governments. The case outcome will influence how companies like NSO Group's U.S. operations, various spyware vendors, and surveillance infrastructure providers assess legal risks when serving authoritarian markets. The precedent could also affect how companies structure international operations to limit U.S. court jurisdiction.
