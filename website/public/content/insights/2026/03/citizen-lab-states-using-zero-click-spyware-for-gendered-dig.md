# Citizen Lab: States Using Zero-Click Spyware for Gendered Digital Repression

**TL;DR:** Citizen Lab research reveals how authoritarian states are using zero-click spyware and data brokers to target women activists across borders with gender-specific harassment tactics.

## Key Points

- Citizen Lab published report on gendered dimensions of digital transnational repression in late 2024
- States use zero-click spyware and data broker information to target women human rights defenders abroad
- Attacks exploit patriarchal norms, using threats about women's bodies/sexuality to silence activists
- Digital repression causes psychological distress, leading to self-censorship and withdrawal from public life
- Autocrats can target women activists 'without deploying a lot of resources' through commercial spyware
- Researcher Noura Aljizawi, herself a victim, emphasizes community support as key to building resilience
- Report calls for better support systems for victims of gendered digital repression

## Deep Dive

## Context and Threat Model

This research expands our understanding of how commercial spyware markets enable targeted persecution beyond traditional political surveillance. The Citizen Lab's findings demonstrate that zero-click exploits—which require no user interaction—are being systematically deployed against specific demographic groups using gender as a vector for psychological warfare.

## Technical and Economic Dimensions

The report highlights two key enablers: commercial spyware accessibility and data broker ecosystems. States can now purchase surveillance capabilities rather than develop them internally, lowering barriers to transnational repression. The combination of zero-click exploits with purchased personal data creates a force multiplier for harassment campaigns that would previously require significant human intelligence resources.

## Implications for Defense

The gendered nature of these attacks reveals how technical surveillance tools are being weaponized through social engineering at scale. This suggests that purely technical defenses (secure communications, device hardening) must be complemented by community-based resilience strategies. The research also indicates that commercial spyware vendors are either willingly or negligently enabling systematic human rights abuses.

## What Builders Should Consider

Developers of privacy tools need to account for the psychological warfare dimension of state surveillance, not just the technical interception. This includes designing tools that help users maintain operational security while building community connections, and considering how their tools might be used in coordinated harassment campaigns rather than isolated surveillance scenarios.
