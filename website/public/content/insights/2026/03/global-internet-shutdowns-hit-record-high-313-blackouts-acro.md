# Global Internet Shutdowns Hit Record High: 313 Blackouts Across 52 Countries in 2025

**TL;DR:** Access Now reports 2025 saw 313 internet shutdowns across 52 countries—a record high with no shutdown-free days. Myanmar led with 95 shutdowns, while authorities increasingly target satellite internet systems.

## Key Points

- 313 internet shutdowns documented across 52 countries in 2025, up from 304 in 2024 [Access Now]
- Myanmar recorded highest number with 95 shutdowns for second consecutive year
- Seven first-time offenders including Albania, Cambodia, and the United States
- Conflict was primary trigger for 125 shutdowns (40% of total) across 14 countries
- 70 shutdowns coincided with grave human rights abuses including torture and war crimes in 21 countries
- Sharp increase in satellite internet targeting: 14 LEO satellite shutdowns vs 4 in 2024
- Satellite shutdowns occurred in Chad, Equatorial Guinea, Iran, Myanmar, Syria, Tanzania, Yemen
- 100 countries worldwide have now experienced shutdowns since tracking began in 2016
- Bangladesh took steps toward outlawing shutdowns; International Criminal Court recognized shutdown-crime link

## Deep Dive

## Context and Escalation

The 2025 data represents a troubling acceleration in digital authoritarianism, with authorities worldwide normalizing internet shutdowns as tools of control. The fact that not a single day passed without at least one shutdown globally indicates systematic, coordinated digital repression rather than isolated incidents. Myanmar's position as the worst offender for two consecutive years reflects the military junta's reliance on information control to maintain power following the 2021 coup.

## Technical Threat Evolution

The 250% increase in satellite internet shutdowns (from 4 to 14) marks a critical escalation in censorship capabilities. Authorities are specifically targeting Low Earth Orbit (LEO) systems like Starlink, which were previously considered censorship-resistant alternatives to terrestrial internet infrastructure. This development suggests governments are adapting their technical capabilities to counter emerging freedom tech solutions, making traditional backup communication methods insufficient.

## Implications for Freedom Tech Development

The systematic targeting of both terrestrial and satellite communications creates an urgent need for truly decentralized, mesh-based communication networks that cannot be shut down from central points of control. The correlation between shutdowns and human rights abuses (70 cases across 21 countries) demonstrates that communication blackouts are increasingly used to conceal atrocities, making censorship-resistant tools literal lifelines for vulnerable populations.

## Strategic Considerations

The inclusion of first-time offenders like the United States signals that shutdown tactics are spreading beyond traditional authoritarian contexts. This normalization trend, combined with the technical evolution toward satellite targeting, suggests that freedom tech builders must prepare for an environment where all centralized communication infrastructure is potentially compromised. The International Criminal Court's recognition of shutdown-atrocity links provides a legal framework that could be leveraged to challenge these practices.
