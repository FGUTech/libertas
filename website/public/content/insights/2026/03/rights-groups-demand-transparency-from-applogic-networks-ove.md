# Rights Groups Demand Transparency from AppLogic Networks Over Egypt Censorship Tech

**TL;DR:** Human rights orgs are demanding AppLogic Networks prove they've actually withdrawn deep packet inspection tech from Egypt after years of facilitating mass censorship of 630+ websites including independent media.

## Key Points

- AppLogic Networks (formerly Sandvine) committed to exiting Egypt by Dec 31, 2025 after being placed on US Entity List
- Company's deep packet inspection technology documented as enabling mass censorship of 630+ websites in Egypt
- Independent media outlets like Zawia3 continue being blocked even after claimed company withdrawal
- Technical research from Citizen Lab and Egyptian org Masaar traced censorship to AppLogic's DPI technology
- Company cancelled engagement meetings with Egyptian civil society citing 'staff safety concerns'
- Rights groups demand proof of complete hardware removal and disclosure of 1% profit donation to affected orgs
- Pattern shows gap between corporate human rights commitments and actual transparency/remediation

## Deep Dive

## Context and Background

AppLogic Networks (formerly Sandvine) was placed on the US Department of Commerce's Entity List for providing technology that facilitated digital repression. As part of reforms to be removed from this list, the company committed to exiting Egypt and other non-democratic jurisdictions by December 31, 2025, and pledged to donate 1% of profits to internet freedom organizations.

## Technical Breakdown

The core issue involves deep packet inspection (DPI) technology that enables granular network traffic analysis and blocking. Technical research from Citizen Lab and Egyptian organization Masaar has directly traced mass website censorship in Egypt to AppLogic's DPI equipment. This technology allows authorities to identify and block specific websites, applications, or communication patterns in real-time, making it particularly effective for targeting independent media and civil society organizations.

## Ongoing Threat Model Implications

The continued blocking of websites like Zawia3 (and its backup domain zawia3.net) after AppLogic's claimed withdrawal raises critical questions about hardware persistence and operational handoffs. Even if AppLogic has formally exited, their equipment may remain operational under Egyptian control, or the capabilities may have been transferred to other vendors. This highlights a key challenge in the digital rights space: corporate exits from authoritarian markets don't automatically disable existing surveillance/censorship infrastructure.

## What Builders Should Consider

This case demonstrates the importance of technical kill switches and remote disabling capabilities in surveillance/DPI technology. The fact that 630+ websites remain blocked suggests either incomplete withdrawal or successful technology transfer. Freedom tech builders should study how censorship systems persist beyond vendor relationships and develop circumvention tools specifically designed to counter DPI-based blocking. The targeting of backup domains (zawia3.net) also shows the need for more sophisticated domain-fronting and anti-censorship strategies.
