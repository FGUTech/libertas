# Ugandan Journalist Disappeared After Discussing Struck-Down Surveillance Laws

**TL;DR:** Journalist Mukose Arnold Anthony was forcibly disappeared by plainclothes security after discussing Constitutional Court ruling that nullified Computer Misuse Act sections used to target activists.

## Key Points

- Mukose Arnold Anthony of Alternative Digitalk forcibly taken by plainclothes men after radio broadcast on March 22
- Had just discussed Constitutional Court's March 17 ruling nullifying Computer Misuse Act sections targeting journalists
- Joint Anti-Terrorism Task Force reportedly involved, searched his home and seized electronic devices
- Police seized ID, passport, modem, laptop, and smartwatches from his residence
- Part of pattern: JATF accused of illegally detaining 100+ people, some tortured to death
- Alternative Digitalk previously raided in 2022 under now-annulled Computer Misuse Act provisions
- Police confirmed holding Mukose in custody, will arraign in court 'in due course'

## Deep Dive

## Context and Pattern

This incident represents a chilling escalation in Uganda's crackdown on media freedom, particularly targeting those covering digital rights and legal developments. The timing is significant—Mukose disappeared after discussing a Constitutional Court victory that struck down key surveillance and censorship tools. This suggests authorities are retaliating against coverage of legal setbacks to their repressive digital apparatus.

## Technical and Operational Security Implications

The seizure of electronic devices (modem, laptop, smartwatches) indicates authorities are conducting digital forensics to map Mukose's communications and sources. The involvement of the Joint Anti-Terrorism Task Force—Uganda's multi-agency surveillance apparatus—demonstrates how anti-terrorism infrastructure is being weaponized against journalists. The search occurred days after the initial detention, suggesting systematic intelligence gathering rather than emergency response.

## Broader Threat Landscape

Human Rights Watch has documented the JATF's involvement in 100+ illegal detentions, with some victims tortured to death. High-profile cases include opposition figures and activists, with Museveni's son Muhoozi publicly boasting about holding detainees "in my basement." This creates a climate of terror around any coverage of surveillance, digital rights, or government overreach.

## What This Means for Freedom Tech

The targeting of journalists covering legal victories against surveillance laws signals that Uganda views independent media as a critical threat to its digital control agenda. The Constitutional Court's nullification of Computer Misuse Act sections was a significant win for digital rights—the government's response shows they will use extrajudicial means when legal tools are stripped away. This pattern is likely to accelerate as more African courts challenge overbroad cybercrime laws.
