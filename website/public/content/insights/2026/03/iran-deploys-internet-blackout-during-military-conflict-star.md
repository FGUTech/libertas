# Iran Deploys Internet Blackout During Military Conflict; Starlink Terminals Targeted

**TL;DR:** Iran shut down internet access during Operation Epic Fury military strikes, while regime reportedly targets Iranians using smuggled Starlink terminals for circumvention.

## Key Points

- Iranian authorities implemented internet shutdown as military strikes began during Operation Epic Fury
- Trump administration reportedly smuggled thousands of Starlink terminals into Iran prior to conflict
- Iranian regime targeting citizens using Starlink terminals, making satellite internet use punishable by prison
- Freedom House analysis compares to Putin's playbook of information control during Ukraine invasion
- US government VPN programs for circumvention were previously terminated under foreign aid cuts
- Current satellite internet requires trackable receivers that security services can disrupt
- Next-generation direct-to-phone satellite technology exists but remains undeployed at scale
- Pattern shows authoritarian regimes have mastered internet control despite early democratizing expectations

## Deep Dive

## Context and Background

This represents a critical case study in how authoritarian regimes deploy internet shutdowns as a core component of their control strategy during periods of instability. Iran's response mirrors the "authoritarian playbook" that Putin refined in Russia following the 2022 Ukraine invasion - combining internet restrictions with expanded domestic propaganda while cutting off external information sources. The timing during military action demonstrates how internet control has become a standard operational tactic, not just a response to civil unrest.

## Technical Infrastructure and Countermeasures

The smuggling of thousands of Starlink terminals into Iran represents one of the largest known deployments of satellite-based circumvention technology in an authoritarian context. However, the Iranian regime's ability to track and target these terminals reveals the vulnerability of current satellite internet solutions that require dedicated hardware. The regime has proactively criminalized satellite internet use, showing sophisticated anticipation of circumvention attempts. This creates a dangerous operational environment where possession of circumvention tools carries severe legal consequences.

## Implications for Freedom Tech Development

The case highlights critical gaps in current circumvention technology. While direct-to-phone satellite internet could eliminate the trackable hardware problem, regulatory and business constraints prevent deployment where it's most needed. The termination of government-funded VPN programs has left a void in systematic circumvention support. The analysis suggests that without dedicated government investment in freedom tech, commercial solutions will remain inadequate for populations under severe authoritarian control.

## Strategic Considerations for Builders

This situation demonstrates the need for circumvention tools that don't require detectable hardware or create legal liability for users. The Iranian regime's proactive criminalization strategy suggests that freedom tech must be designed with legal deniability in mind. The comparison to historical radio broadcasting success indicates that systematic, government-backed deployment may be necessary to achieve meaningful scale in highly controlled environments.
