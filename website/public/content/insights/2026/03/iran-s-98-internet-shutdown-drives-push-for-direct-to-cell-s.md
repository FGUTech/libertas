# Iran's 98% Internet Shutdown Drives Push for Direct-to-Cell Satellite Tech

**TL;DR:** Iran imposed a near-total internet blackout during conflict, prompting digital rights advocates to urgently call for Direct-to-Cell satellite technology that connects smartphones directly to satellites, bypassing government shutdowns.

## Key Points

- Iran cut internet connectivity by 98% starting February 28, 2026 following U.S.-Israel military attacks
- Access Now condemns the shutdown affecting millions during critical conflict period
- Blackout blocks journalists and human rights defenders from documenting violations
- Information vacuum enables disinformation spread and hampers accountability efforts
- Access Now advocates for Direct-to-Cell (D2C) satellite technology as solution
- D2C connects ordinary smartphones directly to satellites, bypassing censorship
- Civil society coalition calls for accelerated humanitarian deployment of D2C tech
- Pattern continues Iran's history of using shutdowns to control crisis information

## Deep Dive

## Context and Pattern

Iran's latest internet shutdown represents a continuation of authoritarian information control tactics during politically sensitive periods. This 98% connectivity drop follows a well-established pattern where governments disable communications infrastructure precisely when citizens most need access to information and coordination capabilities. The timing—coinciding with military conflict—demonstrates how shutdowns become weapons against civilian populations.

## Technical Solution: Direct-to-Cell Satellites

The advocacy for Direct-to-Cell (D2C) satellite technology represents a significant shift in censorship resistance strategy. Unlike traditional satellite communications requiring specialized equipment, D2C connects ordinary smartphones directly to satellites, dramatically lowering the technical barrier for bypass capabilities. This technology bypasses terrestrial infrastructure entirely, making it theoretically immune to traditional internet shutdowns that target ISPs and cellular networks.

## Strategic Implications for Freedom Tech

The coalition between Access Now, WITNESS, and other civil society organizations signals growing institutional support for satellite-based censorship resistance. Their call for "humanitarian deployment" during the current standards-setting phase suggests an opportunity to embed human rights considerations into the technical architecture. However, the effectiveness of D2C will ultimately depend on satellite coverage, device compatibility, and whether governments attempt to jam satellite signals or criminalize usage.

## What Builders Should Consider

This crisis highlights the critical gap between theoretical censorship resistance tools and deployable solutions during active conflicts. While D2C satellites show promise, current mesh networking, Tor bridges, and other circumvention tools remain largely ineffective against comprehensive shutdowns. The emphasis on "ordinary smartphones" compatibility suggests future freedom tech should prioritize mainstream device support over specialized hardware requirements.
