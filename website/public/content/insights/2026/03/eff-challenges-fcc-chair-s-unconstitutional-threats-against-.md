# EFF Challenges FCC Chair's Unconstitutional Threats Against Broadcasters

**TL;DR:** EFF and civil liberties groups condemn FCC Chair Brendan Carr's threats to revoke broadcast licenses for unfavorable coverage of Trump, calling them unconstitutional censorship attempts.

## Key Points

- EFF joined coalition opposing FCC Chair Brendan Carr's threats against broadcasters
- Carr threatened license revocation for stations airing content critical of Trump administration
- Civil liberties groups argue threats violate First Amendment protections
- Carr claims 'public interest' standard justifies punishing unfavorable news coverage
- Coalition argues 'public interest' has never permitted viewpoint-based punishment
- Carr's 'falsity' allegations target policy disagreements and criticism, not accuracy
- Groups specifically cite Iran War coverage as example of prohibited government line enforcement
- Coalition demands Carr withdraw unconstitutional threats

## Deep Dive

## Constitutional Implications

This represents a direct government attack on press freedom through regulatory capture. While broadcast licensees operate under 'public interest' requirements that allow some content regulation, the First Amendment still constrains the FCC's authority to impose viewpoint-based restrictions. The coalition's letter highlights that the 'public interest' standard has never been interpreted to permit the type of content-based retaliation Carr is threatening.

## Pattern of Regulatory Weaponization

Carr's threats follow a concerning pattern of using regulatory authority to coerce favorable coverage. The coalition identifies three specific targets: subjective policy disagreements, criticism of Trump and his administration, and any coverage that deviates from official government positions on foreign conflicts like the Iran War. This represents a systematic attempt to transform broadcast regulation into a censorship mechanism.

## What This Means for Freedom Tech

While this directly targets traditional broadcast media, it signals broader government willingness to weaponize regulatory frameworks against dissenting voices. The precedent of revoking licenses based on content disagreements could extend to internet infrastructure providers, social platforms, or other regulated communications services. This reinforces the importance of decentralized, regulation-resistant communication systems that cannot be easily targeted through licensing threats.
