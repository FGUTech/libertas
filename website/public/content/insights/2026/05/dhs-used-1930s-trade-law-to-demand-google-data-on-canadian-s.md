# DHS Used 1930s Trade Law to Demand Google Data on Canadian's Anti-ICE Posts

**TL;DR:** US Homeland Security used a 1930s trade law to force Google to surrender data on a Canadian citizen who posted anti-ICE content on X, despite the person not entering the US in over a decade.

## Key Points

- DHS targeted Canadian citizen for posts on X condemning killings of Renee Good and Alex Pretti
- Used obscure 1930s trade law as legal mechanism to compel data disclosure
- Google was forced to surrender user data including activity and location information
- Target hasn't entered US territory in more than 10 years
- Demonstrates cross-border surveillance reach targeting political speech
- Shows creative use of old legal authorities to circumvent modern privacy protections
- Sets precedent for extraterritorial enforcement against foreign critics

## Deep Dive

## Legal Innovation in Surveillance Overreach

This case represents a concerning evolution in how US authorities are weaponizing antiquated laws for modern surveillance operations. The use of a 1930s trade law to compel tech companies to surrender user data demonstrates how legal frameworks designed for entirely different purposes can be repurposed for digital dragnet operations.

## Cross-Border Implications

The targeting of a Canadian citizen who hasn't set foot in the US for over a decade raises serious questions about the extraterritorial reach of US surveillance apparatus. This suggests that simply being a foreign national posting critical content about US government agencies is sufficient justification for data collection under certain legal theories.

## Technical and Operational Concerns

Google's compliance with this demand highlights the vulnerability of centralized platforms to government pressure, even when targeting foreign users for protected political speech. The fact that location data was included in the request suggests comprehensive surveillance rather than limited investigation.

## Precedent and Escalation Risk

This case could establish dangerous precedent for using trade law enforcement as a backdoor to surveillance authorities that might otherwise require higher legal thresholds. Activists and critics worldwide should consider the implications for their digital footprints when engaging with US-based platforms.
