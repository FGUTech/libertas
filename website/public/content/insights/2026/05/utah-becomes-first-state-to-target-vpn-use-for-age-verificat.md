# Utah Becomes First State to Target VPN Use for Age Verification Bypass

**TL;DR:** Utah's new law, effective May 6, 2026, holds companies liable for age verification of users physically in Utah regardless of VPN use, and bans sharing VPN circumvention instructions.

## Key Points

- Utah Senate Bill 73 becomes first state law specifically targeting VPN use to bypass age verification
- Law takes effect May 6, 2026, creating liability for companies serving Utah residents using VPNs
- Companies hosting adult content cannot provide VPN instructions or facilitate geofencing circumvention
- Users physically in Utah subject to age verification regardless of VPN, proxy, or location masking
- Creates 'liability trap' forcing websites to either ban VPN IPs globally or verify all users worldwide
- Technical enforcement deemed nearly impossible due to constantly changing VPN IP addresses
- First Amendment concerns over restricting platforms from sharing information about lawful privacy tools
- Follows pattern of VPN usage surging after age verification laws in multiple states and countries
- Sets dangerous precedent for other states considering similar VPN restrictions

## Deep Dive

## Context and Legal Precedent

Utah's Senate Bill 73 represents the first successful attempt in the United States to specifically regulate VPN usage for circumventing age verification requirements. While Wisconsin initially considered similar provisions, advocacy efforts successfully removed them due to constitutional and technical concerns. The law emerges from a global pattern where age verification mandates consistently drive increased VPN adoption, with documented surges in Florida, Missouri, Texas, the UK, Australia, and Indonesia.

## Technical Implementation Challenges

The law creates what privacy advocates term a "liability trap" by requiring companies to verify the age of users physically located in Utah, regardless of their apparent digital location. This presents insurmountable technical challenges: no comprehensive VPN IP blocklist exists, providers constantly add new addresses, and blocking attempts face immediate workarounds through non-commercial proxies, cloud services, or residential proxies. Companies face an impossible choice between attempting perfect VPN detection (technically infeasible) or implementing global age verification to avoid liability.

## First Amendment and Privacy Implications

SB 73's ban on sharing VPN information raises significant constitutional concerns by restricting truthful information about lawful privacy tools. Unlike proposed total VPN bans, Utah's approach uses a "don't ask, don't tell" enforcement model where liability only triggers when companies actively know users are in Utah and using VPNs. However, this creates uncertainty about compliance obligations and could chill legitimate privacy education.

## Threat Model Evolution

This law signals a shift from targeting content to targeting the privacy tools themselves. Rather than addressing the underlying issues with age verification mandates, lawmakers are expanding the attack surface to include fundamental privacy infrastructure. The precedent threatens to normalize restrictions on VPN usage beyond age verification into broader censorship scenarios, potentially affecting journalists, activists, and abuse survivors who depend on commercial VPN services for essential security.
