# How Journalists Navigate Internet Shutdowns Across Iran, India, Ethiopia, and Turkey

**TL;DR:** Global Voices report documents how journalists work around government-imposed internet shutdowns and targeted censorship across multiple countries, with Iran's recent blackouts affecting thousands of protesters.

## Key Points

- Iran imposed near-total internet shutdown during January 2026 protests, followed by another blackout after US-Israeli strikes in February
- Access Now documented nearly 2,000 internet shutdowns globally between 2016-2024, with numbers rising since 2020
- India maintained 552-day internet blackout in Jammu and Kashmir (2019-2021), affecting 12 million residents
- Ethiopia cut internet access to Tigray region for nearly two years during conflict with TPLF (November 2020-February 2023)
- Turkey employs targeted social media censorship, blocking platforms when content becomes 'annoying for the government'
- Journalists report verification challenges, delayed stories, and inability to access photos/videos during blackouts
- Shutdowns often precede violence against civilians and disrupt essential services beyond journalism
- Organizations like OONI document targeted censorship of specific websites and apps alongside broader shutdowns

## Deep Dive

## Pattern of State Control

This comprehensive report reveals a systematic global pattern where governments deploy internet shutdowns as tools of political control, particularly during conflicts and protests. The Iranian case is especially significant - the January 2026 shutdown during protests that killed thousands (3,000-30,000 depending on sources) demonstrates how digital infrastructure weaponization accompanies physical violence against civilians.

## Technical Methods and Impact

The report identifies multiple technical approaches beyond simple shutdowns: throttling (reducing speeds to 2G levels), targeted app blocking, and social media platform censorship. These graduated responses suggest governments are developing more sophisticated digital control strategies. The 552-day Kashmir shutdown and two-year Tigray blackout show how temporary measures can become extended sieges, fundamentally altering information ecosystems.

## Implications for Freedom Tech

For builders, this data highlights critical gaps in current circumvention tools. Traditional VPNs and proxies fail during complete shutdowns, while throttling attacks defeat bandwidth-dependent solutions. The journalist testimonies reveal specific failure modes: inability to verify information, delayed reporting, loss of visual evidence, and breakdown of coordination networks. The mention of organizations like OONI and NetBlocks as monitoring infrastructure suggests there's growing technical capacity to document these attacks, but limited ability to prevent them.

## What This Signals

The increasing sophistication and frequency of shutdowns (rising since 2020 according to Access Now data) indicates governments view digital infrastructure control as essential to maintaining power during crises. The fact that multiple countries are independently developing similar tactics suggests knowledge sharing or parallel evolution of digital authoritarianism techniques.
