# Major Financial Firms Block Donations to SPLC After DOJ Indictment

**TL;DR:** Fidelity, Vanguard, and Schwab are blocking donor-advised fund donations to SPLC following Trump DOJ indictment, demonstrating financial censorship without due process.

## Key Points

- Fidelity Charitable, Vanguard Charitable, and DAFgiving360 (Schwab) blocking donations to Southern Poverty Law Center
- Financial blockade follows Trump DOJ indictment that House whistleblowers say was 'rushed through' despite weak case
- Donor-advised funds (DAFs) represent critical funding pipeline for nonprofits, managed by major financial firms
- Pattern matches WikiLeaks 2010 financial blockade by Visa/Mastercard that eliminated 95% of revenue
- No evidence of government pressure on financial firms - decisions based on internal terms of service
- San Francisco Foundation continues SPLC funding, citing values over 'political winds'
- Demonstrates how financial intermediaries can silence organizations without court proceedings
- Trump previously signed executive order against 'politicized debanking' but SPLC now facing same treatment

## Deep Dive

## Financial Censorship as Political Weapon

The SPLC case represents a sophisticated evolution in financial censorship tactics. Unlike the 2010 WikiLeaks blockade where government pressure was more overt, here major financial institutions are making unilateral decisions based on internal policies following a politically motivated indictment. This creates plausible deniability while achieving the same silencing effect.

## Technical and Legal Implications

The donor-advised fund ecosystem has become critical infrastructure for nonprofit funding, with firms like Fidelity and Vanguard controlling access to billions in charitable donations. Their ability to unilaterally cut funding streams demonstrates the centralized chokepoints that exist in the current financial system. While courts have recognized dangers when government directly pressures financial companies (Backpage.com v. Dart, NRA v. Vullo), private company decisions based on internal terms create a legal gray area that's harder to challenge.

## Threat Model for Activists

This case illustrates how financial exclusion operates as a pre-trial punishment that can destroy organizations before they have their day in court. The SPLC faces a 'double jeopardy' situation - fighting both a politicized prosecution and financial strangulation simultaneously. For builders, this highlights the critical need for censorship-resistant funding mechanisms that can't be controlled by centralized financial intermediaries.

## What This Means for Freedom Tech

The SPLC blockade demonstrates that financial censorship is not limited to crypto projects or fringe organizations - even established civil rights groups with mainstream support can be targeted. This validates the freedom tech thesis that decentralized, permissionless financial infrastructure is essential for preserving civil society. The fact that this is happening under an administration that previously criticized 'politicized debanking' shows how quickly political winds can shift.
