# EFF Opposes Insufficient FISA 702 Reauthorization Bill Lacking Warrant Requirements

**TL;DR:** EFF condemns Speaker Johnson's Foreign Intelligence Accountability Act as inadequate reform that fails to require warrants for FBI searches of Americans' communications collected under Section 702 surveillance.

## Key Points

- Speaker Johnson introduced Foreign Intelligence Accountability Act days before Section 702 expires
- Bill fails to require warrants for FBI searches of Americans' communications
- Creates civil liberties officer to review queries after surveillance occurs
- Prohibits targeting US persons but current law already does this
- EFF calls it a 'fig leaf' and 'smokescreen' with no real privacy protections
- Section 702 allows warrantless collection of Americans' communications as 'incidental'
- Bill includes no new transparency requirements or meaningful accountability
- EFF urges Congress to reject bill and demand real warrant requirements

## Deep Dive

## Context and Stakes

Section 702 of FISA represents one of the most expansive surveillance authorities in US law, allowing intelligence agencies to collect communications of foreign targets that routinely sweep up Americans' data. The program requires periodic reauthorization, creating opportunities for reform that privacy advocates have consistently pushed for but rarely achieved. The current debate occurs against a backdrop of documented FBI abuses, including hundreds of thousands of improper queries of Americans' data.

## Technical and Legal Analysis

The core issue isn't the collection itself but the 'backdoor searches' - FBI agents querying the collected data using US person identifiers without judicial oversight. Current law technically prohibits 'targeting' Americans, but the 'incidental collection' loophole renders this meaningless when Americans communicate with foreign contacts or use services hosted abroad. Johnson's bill maintains this fundamental architecture while adding cosmetic oversight.

## Threat Model Implications

For privacy-focused builders, this represents a continuation of the existing threat environment where any communication that touches foreign infrastructure or contacts remains subject to warrantless government access. The proposed 'reform' actually codifies the status quo while providing political cover for lawmakers. The post-hoc review mechanism offers no protection since damage occurs at the point of access, not review.

## Strategic Considerations

EFF's characterization of this as a 'fig leaf' reflects the broader pattern of surveillance reform theater - minor procedural changes presented as meaningful protection. The timing, introduced just days before expiration, suggests an attempt to force acceptance of inadequate measures. For freedom tech advocates, this reinforces the importance of technical solutions that assume comprehensive state surveillance rather than relying on policy protections.
