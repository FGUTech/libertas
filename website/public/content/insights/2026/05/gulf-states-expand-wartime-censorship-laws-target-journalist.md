# Gulf States Expand Wartime Censorship Laws, Target Journalists for Routine Reporting

**TL;DR:** Committee to Protect Journalists documents systematic crackdown on press freedom across Gulf states since Iran war began, with journalists detained for sharing verified public information under vague 'national security' laws.

## Key Points

- CPJ documented widespread arrests and intimidation of journalists across Kuwait, Bahrain, UAE, Saudi Arabia, and Qatar since Iran war started in February 2026
- Journalists detained for routine activities like photographing public events, sharing verified information about missile strikes and military incidents
- Authorities using vague charges like 'spreading false information,' 'harming national security,' and cybercrime violations to prosecute media workers
- Kuwait enacted Decree-Law No. 13 imposing up to 10 years prison for sharing military information deemed to undermine 'public confidence'
- Award-winning journalist Ahmed Shihab-Eldin held 52 days in Kuwait for sharing verified video of U.S. fighter jet crash
- Many cases go unreported due to fear of retaliation, creating climate of self-censorship among journalists
- CPJ warns wartime restrictions may become permanent, citing historical precedent of emergency laws outliving their original purpose
- Restrictions go beyond legitimate operational security to enforce 'ideological conformity' and protect government narratives

## Deep Dive

## Historical Context and Permanence Risk

The current press freedom crackdown follows a disturbing historical pattern in the Gulf region where emergency powers become permanent fixtures of authoritarian control. CPJ specifically cites Bahrain's 2011 State of National Safety and Saudi Arabia's 2014 Counter-Terrorism Law as examples of crisis legislation that persisted long after emergencies ended, permanently expanding state censorship capabilities. This 'emergency ratchet' effect represents a fundamental threat to information freedom in the region.

## Technical and Legal Architecture

The enforcement mechanisms show sophisticated coordination across Gulf states, utilizing broadly defined cybercrime laws and anti-rumor legislation to criminalize information sharing. Kuwait's Decree-Law No. 13 and specialized security courts demonstrate how governments rapidly deploy legal infrastructure to control information flows. The vague language around 'undermining public confidence' and 'state narratives' creates expansive prosecutorial discretion that extends far beyond legitimate operational security concerns.

## Information Control vs. Legitimate Security

While Gulf states argue that controlling battle damage footage prevents adversaries from using social media as real-time targeting intelligence, the documented cases show authorities targeting verified, publicly available information rather than operational secrets. This distinction is crucial for freedom tech builders: the crackdown aims at narrative control and social compliance rather than protecting military operations. The economic motivation—protecting the 'safety brand' for foreign investment—reveals how information control serves broader authoritarian objectives beyond wartime security.

## Implications for Communication Infrastructure

The systematic nature of this crackdown, combined with journalists' fear-driven self-censorship, demonstrates the fragility of open information systems under authoritarian pressure. The fact that many cases go unreported suggests the actual scale of suppression exceeds documented incidents, indicating successful intimidation campaigns that achieve censorship without formal prosecution.
