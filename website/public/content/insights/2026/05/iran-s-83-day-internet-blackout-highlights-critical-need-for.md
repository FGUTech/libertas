# Iran's 83-Day Internet Blackout Highlights Critical Need for Censorship-Resistant Comms

**TL;DR:** Iran has imposed a record-breaking 83-day nationwide internet blackout to hide media crackdowns, creating an information vacuum that demonstrates the urgent need for mesh networks and satellite communications.

## Key Points

- Iran imposed 83-day nationwide internet blackout - longest and most severe ever recorded [NetBlocks]
- Committee to Protect Journalists (CPJ) and Reporters Without Borders (RSF) issued joint warning
- Blackout specifically targets press freedom, making secure communications impossible for journalists
- Iranian-American journalist Reza Valizadeh detained in Evin Prison with unknown status
- Information vacuum prevents verification of arrests and documentation of human rights abuses
- Demonstrates real-world scenario where freedom tech tools become critical infrastructure
- Press freedom groups calling for immediate restoration of internet access and journalist releases

## Deep Dive

## Unprecedented Scale of Information Control

Iran's 83-day internet blackout represents the most extreme case of state-level information control documented by monitoring organizations. According to NetBlocks, this 12-week shutdown surpasses all previous Iranian internet restrictions, effectively isolating 85 million people from global communications networks. The systematic nature of this blackout demonstrates how authoritarian regimes can weaponize internet infrastructure against civil society.

## Technical and Operational Implications

The blackout has severed journalists from secure communication channels, encrypted messaging apps, and verification tools that modern reporting depends on. This creates a perfect case study for why decentralized, satellite-based, and mesh networking solutions are essential for press freedom. The inability of international organizations to confirm arrests or document abuses highlights the critical gap that censorship-resistant technologies must fill.

## Freedom Tech Requirements

This scenario validates the threat models that drive mesh networking projects, satellite communication systems, and offline-first journalism tools. When traditional internet infrastructure becomes a weapon of state control, alternative communication pathways become literal lifelines for journalists, activists, and ordinary citizens. The Iran situation demonstrates that freedom tech isn't just theoretical - it's an urgent operational requirement for information freedom.

## Broader Implications for Builders

The Iranian blackout provides real-world validation for freedom tech development priorities. Tools that can operate without traditional internet infrastructure, provide secure communications under surveillance conditions, and enable information verification in hostile environments are no longer edge cases - they're core requirements for defending press freedom in an increasingly authoritarian world.
