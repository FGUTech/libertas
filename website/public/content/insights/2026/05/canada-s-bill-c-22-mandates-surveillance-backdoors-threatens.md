# Canada's Bill C-22 Mandates Surveillance Backdoors, Threatens Encryption

**TL;DR:** Canada's Bill C-22 would force tech companies to build surveillance backdoors, retain metadata for a year, and could ban strong encryption despite vague 'systemic vulnerability' exceptions.

## Key Points

- Bill C-22 (Lawful Access Act) is repackaged version of failed Bill C-2 surveillance legislation
- Mandates digital services retain user metadata for full year, including telecoms and messaging apps
- Allows Minister of Public Safety to demand backdoors as long as they don't create 'systemic vulnerabilities'
- Definitions of 'systemic vulnerabilities' and 'encryption' are deliberately vague, creating regulatory loopholes
- Companies would be banned from publicly disclosing existence of backdoor orders
- Apple and Meta have publicly opposed the bill, citing encryption concerns
- Mirrors UK's successful pressure campaign that forced Apple to disable Advanced Data Protection
- US House committees sent joint letter warning Canada about backdoor dangers

## Deep Dive

## Context and Escalation Pattern

Bill C-22 represents the second attempt by Canadian authorities to implement comprehensive surveillance infrastructure after Bill C-2 failed due to privacy community backlash. This follows a concerning international pattern where governments are coordinating pressure on encryption, as evidenced by the UK's successful campaign to force Apple to disable Advanced Data Protection for British users in 2025.

## Technical Threat Model

The bill's core danger lies in its deliberately ambiguous language around 'systemic vulnerabilities.' By allowing the Minister of Public Safety to mandate backdoors while claiming they won't introduce systemic risks, the legislation creates a regulatory framework for gradually eroding encryption standards. The mandatory metadata retention requirement would create vast surveillance databases covering communication patterns, location data, and behavioral analytics - exactly the infrastructure needed to support broader surveillance operations.

## Precedent and International Implications

The Salt Typhoon hack of 2024 demonstrated how law enforcement backdoors become attack vectors for hostile actors, yet Canada is proceeding with similar infrastructure requirements. The fact that both Apple and Meta are publicly opposing this legislation - something they rarely do - signals genuine concern about technical feasibility and security implications. The US congressional intervention suggests recognition that Canadian surveillance infrastructure could impact American users and companies.

## Strategic Response Considerations

For freedom tech builders, C-22 represents a critical test case for resistance to government backdoor mandates. The bill's passage could establish precedent for other Five Eyes nations and provide a template for pressuring encryption providers. The prohibition on companies disclosing backdoor orders creates additional operational security challenges for users trying to assess platform integrity.
