# Tunisia Escalates Media Crackdown Beyond Individual Prosecutions to Target Infrastructure

**TL;DR:** Tunisia is dismantling independent media infrastructure through banking freezes, dissolution proceedings, and financial crime charges while continuing to jail journalists under Decree-Law 54.

## Key Points

- Tunisian authorities expanding beyond Decree-Law 54 to use financial crime charges, banking restrictions, and dissolution proceedings against media outlets
- Journalists Sonia Dahmani (18 months) and Ghassen Ben Khelifa (2 years) sentenced to prison terms that can be enforced at any moment as intimidation tactic
- Radio journalists Mourad Zghidi and Borhen Bsaies hit with money laundering charges after initial Decree 54 prosecutions, sentenced to 3.5 years
- Independent outlet Al Khatt (behind Inkyfada) facing dissolution proceedings after months of banking restrictions blocking funds
- Multiple outlets including Tumedia and Nawaat experiencing coordinated financial pressure and administrative harassment
- Tunisian League for Human Rights, Nobel Peace Prize winner, suspended for one month showing broader civil society targeting
- Strategy aims to dismantle infrastructure supporting independent journalism, not just punish individual journalists
- Tunisia's reversal from post-Arab Spring press freedom gains demonstrates how quickly democratic spaces can collapse

## Deep Dive

## Context and Escalation

Tunisia's systematic assault on independent media represents a dramatic reversal from its post-2011 reputation as the Arab Spring's lone democratic success story. Under President Kais Saied, what began with Decree-Law 54 prosecutions has evolved into a multi-vector campaign targeting the financial and organizational infrastructure that sustains independent journalism. The Committee to Protect Journalists documents how authorities now deploy banking freezes, administrative audits, dissolution proceedings, and financial crime charges alongside traditional speech prosecutions.

## Technical and Financial Warfare

The banking restrictions targeting outlets like Al Khatt and Tumedia represent a sophisticated form of financial censorship. By blocking international transfers from donors without explanation and demanding extensive documentation about funding sources, authorities create operational paralysis without formal closure orders. This approach is particularly effective because it starves outlets of resources while maintaining plausible deniability about press freedom violations.

## Implications for Freedom Tech

Tunisia's crackdown illustrates exactly why censorship-resistant financial infrastructure and communications tools are critical. When traditional banking systems become weapons of censorship and journalists face constant threat of detention, the need for decentralized alternatives becomes urgent. The targeting of civil society organizations alongside media outlets suggests authorities understand that supporting infrastructure—not just content—must be controlled to achieve total information dominance.

## What Builders Should Consider

This case study demonstrates how quickly democratic gains can be reversed and why freedom tech infrastructure must be designed for hostile regulatory environments from the start. The combination of legal harassment, financial pressure, and administrative burden shows why tools need to operate independently of traditional banking systems and regulatory frameworks that can be weaponized by authoritarian actors.
