# Human Rights Watch: EU Surveillance Tech Exports Enable Global Rights Violations

**TL;DR:** Human Rights Watch report reveals EU's 2021 Dual-Use Regulation is failing to prevent surveillance technology exports to governments that use them to spy on activists and journalists.

## Key Points

- 54-page HRW report documents EU failure to control surveillance technology exports to rights violators
- EU's 2021 Dual-Use Regulation intended to prevent exports to governments likely to violate human rights
- Bulgaria exported intrusion software and telecom interception to Azerbaijan (2022), Poland to Rwanda (2023)
- European Commission weakened transparency requirements, obfuscating export destinations and technologies
- EU hosts major surveillance tech developers but individual export decisions left to member states
- Commission claims limited companies justify hiding details due to 'commercial confidentiality'
- HRW obtained data from nearly half of EU countries through freedom of information requests
- Report recommends stronger due diligence requirements and meaningful transparency for surveillance exports

## Deep Dive

## Regulatory Failure in Practice

The EU's landmark Dual-Use Regulation, adopted in 2021, was designed to prevent European surveillance technology from facilitating human rights abuses globally. However, Human Rights Watch's comprehensive analysis reveals systematic implementation failures. The European Commission has reinterpreted transparency obligations in ways that undermine the regulation's core purpose, making it nearly impossible to assess whether controls are working [Source].

## Documented Export Patterns

The report provides concrete evidence of problematic exports: Bulgaria licensed intrusion software and telecommunication interception systems to Azerbaijan in 2022, while Poland exported telecom interception capabilities to Rwanda in 2023 [Source]. Both recipient countries have well-documented histories of using surveillance technology to target activists, journalists, and civil society. This pattern suggests the regulation's human rights risk assessment mechanisms are either inadequate or not being properly implemented.

## Technical and Policy Implications

The Commission's decision to aggregate and obfuscate export data undermines accountability mechanisms that civil society and researchers need to monitor dual-use technology flows. By citing 'commercial confidentiality' concerns about the limited number of companies in the surveillance export market, the Commission has essentially created a transparency blackout [Source]. This approach directly contradicts the regulation's stated goals of preventing technology-enabled repression.

## Critical Juncture for Reform

With the European Commission required to evaluate the Dual-Use Regulation later in 2026, this represents a crucial opportunity to strengthen controls. The report's recommendations for enhanced due diligence requirements, real transparency mandates, and meaningful stakeholder participation could significantly impact global surveillance technology flows if implemented. For freedom tech builders, understanding these export patterns is essential for threat modeling and developing countermeasures against state-sponsored surveillance capabilities.
