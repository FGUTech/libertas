# Signal Threatens to Exit Canada Over Encryption-Breaking Bill C-22

**TL;DR:** Signal's VP of Strategy says the encrypted messaging app would rather leave Canada than comply with Bill C-22, which could force backdoors into end-to-end encryption.

## Key Points

- Signal VP of Strategy states company would exit Canada rather than comply with Bill C-22
- Bill C-22 is Canada's proposed 'lawful access' legislation targeting encrypted communications
- Legislation could force messaging platforms to implement backdoors or weaken encryption
- Signal's position follows pattern of principled resistance to encryption-breaking laws
- Move would impact Canadian users' access to Signal's secure messaging platform
- Sets precedent for how privacy-focused companies respond to government overreach
- Demonstrates tension between state surveillance goals and encryption integrity

## Deep Dive

## Context

Canada's Bill C-22 represents the latest government attempt to mandate 'lawful access' to encrypted communications, joining similar legislative efforts in the EU, UK, and other jurisdictions. Signal's threat to exit Canada rather than compromise its encryption follows the company's established pattern of principled resistance to surveillance mandates.

## Technical Implications

The core issue is that true end-to-end encryption cannot coexist with reliable government backdoors. Any mechanism that allows 'lawful access' creates vulnerabilities that can be exploited by malicious actors. Signal's position reflects the technical reality that secure communications and mandated government access are fundamentally incompatible.

## Strategic Significance

Signal's willingness to abandon the Canadian market rather than compromise its security model sends a strong signal to other governments considering similar legislation. This approach contrasts with tech giants who often attempt compliance through technical workarounds or legal challenges. For the freedom tech ecosystem, Signal's stance demonstrates that principled resistance to surveillance overreach remains possible.

## What This Means for Builders

The situation highlights the importance of building decentralized alternatives that cannot be easily regulated or blocked by single jurisdictions. It also underscores the value of protocols and tools that can operate independently of corporate entities that governments can pressure or exclude from markets.
