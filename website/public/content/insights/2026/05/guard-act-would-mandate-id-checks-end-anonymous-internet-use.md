# GUARD Act Would Mandate ID Checks, End Anonymous Internet Use

**TL;DR:** The GUARD Act, framed as AI safety for kids, would require age verification for all users of AI-powered tools, effectively ending anonymous internet use and creating mass surveillance infrastructure.

## Key Points

- Congress moving quickly on GUARD Act with key vote expected this week
- Bill requires 'reasonable age verification' using government ID or biometric data for all users of AI-powered systems
- Broad definitions would cover search engines, customer service chats, and homework help tools
- Minors would be banned from 'AI companions' - any chatbot designed to facilitate 'interpersonal or emotional interaction'
- Companies face $100,000 per violation penalties, incentivizing overblocking
- Age verification must be ongoing, not one-time, creating persistent identity tracking
- Would block anonymous/pseudonymous internet use and create surveillance infrastructure
- Millions without current government ID would be shut out of basic online services

## Deep Dive

## Context and Scope

The GUARD Act represents one of the most significant threats to anonymous internet use in recent years. While framed as protecting children from harmful AI interactions, the bill's broad definitions would capture virtually any AI-powered system that generates conversational responses. This includes search engines with AI features, customer service chatbots, and educational tools - essentially creating a universal age verification mandate for much of the modern internet.

## Technical and Privacy Implications

The bill's 'reasonable age verification' requirement goes far beyond simple checkbox confirmations, mandating systems that can verify individual identity through government IDs, financial data, or biometric identifiers. This creates several critical vulnerabilities: centralized databases of sensitive identity information become high-value targets for attackers, as demonstrated by previous breaches of age verification companies. The ongoing verification requirement means this isn't a one-time privacy sacrifice but persistent identity tracking across services.

## Enforcement and Compliance Dynamics

With penalties of $100,000 per violation and vague definitions around 'emotional interaction,' companies face a classic overblocking scenario. The combination of steep financial liability and legal uncertainty will drive risk-averse compliance strategies. Smaller developers who can't absorb compliance costs and legal risk will be pushed out, further consolidating power among the largest tech companies who can build and maintain age verification infrastructure.

## What This Means for Freedom Tech

This bill represents a fundamental shift toward an identity-required internet, undermining decades of pseudonymous and anonymous online interaction. For freedom tech builders, this creates both immediate compliance challenges and a broader precedent for mandatory identity verification. The surveillance infrastructure created for 'child safety' would inevitably expand to other use cases, following the familiar pattern of exceptional measures becoming normalized surveillance tools.
