# Signal Threatens Canada Exit Over Lawful Access Bill C-22

**TL;DR:** Signal and Meta oppose Canada's Bill C-22, which could mandate metadata collection for messaging apps. Signal threatens to pull out of Canada if forced to comply with the surveillance requirements.

## Key Points

- Canada's proposed Bill C-22 targets encrypted messaging apps with lawful access requirements
- Signal explicitly threatens to exit Canadian market rather than comply
- Meta also expressing opposition to the proposed legislation
- Bill could mandate forced metadata collection from messaging platforms
- Government officials reportedly avoiding commitments to protect encryption
- Citizen Lab researcher Kate Robertson warns of threats to encrypted communications
- Stakes involve global precedent for government pressure on encrypted messaging
- Pattern matches Signal's previous exit threats in other jurisdictions

## Deep Dive

## Context

Canada's Bill C-22 represents another front in the global battle over lawful access to encrypted communications. The legislation follows similar efforts in the EU, UK, and other Five Eyes nations to compel tech companies to provide government access to user communications. Signal's threat to exit Canada mirrors its previous stance when faced with similar requirements in other jurisdictions.

## Technical and Policy Implications

The bill's focus on metadata collection is particularly concerning for privacy advocates. Even without accessing message content, metadata reveals communication patterns, contact networks, and behavioral data that can be highly revealing. For encrypted messaging apps built on privacy-by-design principles, implementing such collection capabilities would fundamentally compromise their security architecture.

## Strategic Considerations

Signal's public threat serves multiple purposes: it demonstrates the company's commitment to its privacy principles, applies political pressure on Canadian lawmakers, and signals to other governments considering similar legislation. However, this approach also highlights the vulnerability of centralized messaging platforms to jurisdictional pressure.

## What This Means for Builders

This conflict underscores the importance of developing truly decentralized communication tools that cannot be pressured through corporate intermediaries. It also demonstrates the value of building systems that are technically incapable of complying with surveillance mandates, rather than relying on corporate resistance alone.
