# Utah Becomes First State to Regulate VPN Use for Age Verification Bypass

**TL;DR:** Utah's new law, effective May 6, 2026, targets VPN usage to circumvent age verification, requiring companies to verify ages based on physical location regardless of VPN use and banning instruction sharing.

## Key Points

- Utah Senate Bill 73 goes into effect May 6, 2026, first state law targeting VPN use for age-verification bypass
- Law deems users in Utah subject to verification regardless of VPN/proxy use to mask location
- Prohibits adult content sites from providing VPN instructions or circumvention methods
- Creates 'liability trap' forcing sites to either ban VPN IPs globally or implement universal age verification
- Raises First Amendment concerns by restricting truthful information about lawful privacy tools
- Technical implementation deemed impossible due to constantly changing VPN IP addresses
- Sets dangerous precedent after similar provisions removed from Wisconsin bill due to advocacy
- Part of broader 2% tax on adult content revenues, with VPN provisions taking immediate effect

## Deep Dive

## Context and Escalation

Utah's approach represents a significant escalation in the government response to VPN adoption following age verification laws. Rather than recognizing that mass surveillance mandates drive VPN usage surges (as documented in Florida, Missouri, Texas, UK, Australia, and Indonesia), Utah legislators have decided to target the privacy tools themselves. This marks uncharted territory in US digital rights policy, with Utah becoming the first state to successfully implement VPN-targeting provisions after Wisconsin advocates forced removal of similar language.

## Technical Implementation Challenges

The law creates what privacy experts call a "liability trap" through technically impossible requirements. Companies must verify the age of anyone physically in Utah, even when using VPNs, but reliable VPN detection is technically infeasible. VPN providers constantly add new IP addresses, no comprehensive blocklist exists, and motivated users can easily transition to non-commercial proxies, cloud service tunnels, or residential proxies indistinguishable from home traffic. This forces companies into an impossible choice: attempt universal VPN blocking (affecting global users) or implement universal age verification.

## Precedent and Rights Implications

The law's "don't ask, don't tell" enforcement creates a chilling effect on privacy tool usage while raising serious First Amendment concerns. By prohibiting platforms from sharing truthful information about lawful VPN usage, the state is restricting speech about privacy tools. The precedent of holding companies liable for users' physical locations regardless of technical privacy measures fundamentally undermines the expectation that privacy tools provide protection from surveillance. This approach could easily expand to other states and use cases beyond age verification.

## Strategic Implications for Privacy Infrastructure

This represents a shift from indirect pressure on privacy tools to direct legal targeting. The law acknowledges that previous age-verification approaches failed due to VPN adoption, marking government recognition that privacy tools effectively circumvent surveillance infrastructure. For privacy advocates and builders, this signals the need for more robust legal challenges, technical countermeasures, and potentially decentralized approaches that are harder to regulate. The focus on commercial VPN providers suggests that peer-to-peer and self-hosted solutions may become increasingly important for maintaining privacy rights.
