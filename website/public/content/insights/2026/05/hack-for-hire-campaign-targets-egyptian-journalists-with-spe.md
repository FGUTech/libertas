# Hack-for-Hire Campaign Targets Egyptian Journalists with Spear-Phishing Attacks

**TL;DR:** Access Now investigation exposes Asian hack-for-hire group targeting prominent Egyptian journalists and critics Mostafa Al-A'sar and Ahmed Eltantawy through sophisticated spear-phishing attacks attempting to compromise Apple and Google accounts.

## Key Points

- Spear-phishing campaign targeted Egyptian journalists Mostafa Al-A'sar and Ahmed Eltantawy from 2023-2024
- Attackers impersonated trusted contacts and legitimate services including Apple and Signal
- Lookout security firm attributes attacks to hack-for-hire group with ties to Asia
- Infrastructure shows capability to deliver Android spyware with extensive surveillance features
- Both primary targets avoided compromise, but similar attack successfully breached Lebanese journalist's Apple account
- Persistent attack infrastructure uses overlapping domains and hosting for ongoing campaigns
- Pattern connects to broader Egyptian government surveillance using Intellexa Predator spyware
- Investigation collaboration between Access Now, Lookout, SMEX, and Citizen Lab provides comprehensive analysis

## Deep Dive

## Context and Threat Landscape

This investigation reveals a sophisticated transnational surveillance operation targeting high-profile critics of authoritarian regimes in the MENA region. Both targets are prominent Egyptian dissidents: Al-A'sar is an award-winning journalist who spent four years as a political prisoner before fleeing to exile in Canada, while Eltantawy is a former parliamentarian who challenged President al-Sisi before being forced to withdraw and later imprisoned. The timing is significant—attacks occurred during Eltantawy's presidential campaign period, when dozens of his supporters and relatives were arrested.

## Technical Analysis and Infrastructure

The attackers deployed a persistent, professionally-operated infrastructure with overlapping domains and hosting that demonstrates long-term operational capability. The spear-phishing attempts targeted Apple and Google account credentials specifically, which would have provided comprehensive access to victims' digital lives including contacts, communications, and location data. Forensic analysis reveals the infrastructure can deliver Android spyware capable of accessing files, contacts, messages, geolocation, and remotely activating microphones and cameras. The use of fake Signal pages and impersonation of legitimate services shows sophisticated social engineering capabilities designed to exploit trust relationships.

## Attribution and Operational Security Implications

Lookout's independent assessment points to a hack-for-hire group with Asian ties, adding a layer of plausible deniability for state sponsors. While definitive attribution remains elusive, several indicators point toward Egyptian state involvement: the victim profiles, an authentication attempt originating from Egypt, and documented Egyptian government procurement of surveillance technologies. Critically, this campaign runs parallel to confirmed Intellexa Predator spyware targeting of Eltantawy by Egyptian authorities, suggesting a multi-vector approach combining commercial spyware with hack-for-hire services.

## Implications for Freedom Tech Builders

This case demonstrates how authoritarian states are increasingly outsourcing digital repression to third-party contractors, complicating attribution and accountability. The persistent infrastructure and successful compromise of the Lebanese journalist shows these operations achieve real-world impact. The focus on major platform credentials (Apple/Google) rather than deploying custom malware suggests adversaries are adapting to take advantage of centralized digital ecosystems. For builders, this highlights the critical need for decentralized alternatives and improved user education around social engineering attacks that exploit trust relationships.
