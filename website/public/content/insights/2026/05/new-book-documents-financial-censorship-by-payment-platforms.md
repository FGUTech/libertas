# New Book Documents Financial Censorship by Payment Platforms

**TL;DR:** Former EFF director's book 'Transaction Denied' exposes systematic financial censorship by PayPal, Venmo, and other platforms blocking accounts based on speech, with documented cases and successful resistance campaigns.

## Key Points

- Former EFF Activism Director Rainey Reitman published 'Transaction Denied' documenting financial censorship
- Book reveals PayPal/Venmo systematically blocking accounts of teachers, activists, journalists, and politicians
- Persian poetry teacher lost access to funds due to overly broad Iran sanctions interpretation
- Muslim NYC councilwoman had payment blocked for using Bangladeshi restaurant name
- Erotic storytelling platforms and drug legalization advocates repeatedly lose payment access
- Book documents successful advocacy campaigns that restored accounts for Nifty Archive and Smashwords
- Shows how EFF staff helped create Freedom of the Press Foundation to counter payment censorship
- Demonstrates pattern of arbitrary corporate policy and misinterpretation of law driving account closures

## Deep Dive

## The Hidden Infrastructure of Speech Control

Reitman's book exposes a critical vulnerability in digital freedom: the chokepoint control exercised by payment intermediaries. Unlike traditional censorship, financial deplatforming operates with minimal transparency or recourse, effectively silencing individuals by cutting off their economic lifelines. The documented cases span diverse communities—from Persian poetry teachers caught in sanctions overreach to queer storytelling archives losing payment processing—revealing how financial institutions have become unofficial speech arbiters.

## Technical and Legal Mechanisms

The book illuminates how payment platforms use risk-averse interpretations of sanctions law and broad corporate policies to justify account closures. U.S. sanctions targeting Iranian weapons development become pretexts for blocking poetry instruction payments, demonstrating how legal frameworks designed for specific threats expand into general speech suppression. These platforms operate in regulatory gray areas where they can terminate services without judicial oversight, creating a parallel enforcement system outside constitutional protections.

## Successful Resistance Models

Crucially, Reitman documents that organized advocacy can work. The campaigns to restore Stripe access for Nifty Archive Alliance and PayPal access for Smashwords show that coordinated pressure, legal expertise, and public attention can force platform reversals. The creation of Freedom of the Press Foundation represents a more systematic approach—building alternative infrastructure rather than just defending against deplatforming.

## Builder Implications

This documentation arrives as financial censorship intensifies globally. The cases demonstrate why payment sovereignty tools—from cryptocurrency to alternative payment rails—remain critical infrastructure for free expression. Builders should note that the threat extends beyond obvious targets to anyone whose speech or associations might trigger algorithmic flags or policy interpretations. The successful advocacy campaigns provide templates for resistance, but also highlight the need for censorship-resistant payment infrastructure that doesn't require permission from intermediaries.
