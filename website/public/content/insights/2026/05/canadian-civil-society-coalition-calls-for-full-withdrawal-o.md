# Canadian Civil Society Coalition Calls for Full Withdrawal of Surveillance Bill C-22

**TL;DR:** 25 rights organizations led by Citizen Lab demand Canada withdraw Bill C-22, which would mandate year-long metadata retention and force providers to build surveillance backdoors.

## Key Points

- Coalition of 25 rights organizations delivered open letter to PM Mark Carney and Parliament opposing Bill C-22 (Lawful Access Act)
- Citizen Lab researchers Ron Deibert, Noura Aljizawi, Lex Gill, and Kate Robertson among signatories
- Bill would force digital services to record and retain up to one year of detailed metadata on all users
- Legislation would compel 'enormous and poorly defined' set of providers to build surveillance backdoors
- Bill weakens existing rules governing police access to personal information
- Metadata retention would affect both Canadian citizens and international users of Canadian services
- Coalition calls for complete withdrawal rather than amendments to the legislation

## Deep Dive

## Context and Background

Bill C-22 represents a significant expansion of Canada's surveillance apparatus under the banner of 'lawful access.' The legislation builds on previous attempts to expand government surveillance powers in Canada, continuing a pattern seen across Five Eyes nations of pushing for weakened encryption and expanded data retention. The involvement of Citizen Lab—a globally respected digital rights research organization—signals the severity of the privacy implications.

## Technical and Legal Implications

The bill's mandate for year-long metadata retention creates a comprehensive surveillance infrastructure that would capture detailed information about digital communications patterns, location data, and online activities. The 'poorly defined' scope of providers required to implement backdoors creates legal uncertainty while potentially encompassing everything from messaging apps to VPN services. This broad mandate could effectively criminalize many existing privacy tools and force fundamental architectural changes to encrypted communications systems.

## Threat Model Analysis

For freedom tech builders, Bill C-22 represents a blueprint that could be replicated internationally. The metadata retention requirements would create a persistent surveillance database that could be accessed not just by Canadian authorities but potentially shared with international partners. The backdoor mandates threaten the technical foundations of encrypted communications, as any deliberate weakness creates attack vectors for both state and non-state actors.

## Strategic Considerations for Builders

The unified opposition from 25 organizations suggests this legislation faces significant resistance, but builders should prepare for similar proposals elsewhere. The broad definition of covered providers means even decentralized or offshore services could face compliance pressure when serving Canadian users. This highlights the importance of designing systems that can maintain security properties even under hostile regulatory environments.
