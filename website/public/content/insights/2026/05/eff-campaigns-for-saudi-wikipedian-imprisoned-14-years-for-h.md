# EFF Campaigns for Saudi Wikipedian Imprisoned 14 Years for Human Rights Edits

**TL;DR:** Saudi authorities imprisoned Wikipedia contributor Osama Khalid for 14 years after he documented human rights abuses and criticized government surveillance on encrypted platforms.

## Key Points

- Osama Khalid imprisoned in July 2020 for Wikipedia contributions and blogging about Saudi human rights issues
- Original 5-year sentence increased to 32 years, then reduced multiple times to current 14-year term
- His Wikipedia edits covered women's rights activist Loujain al-Hathloul and al-Ha'ir prison conditions
- Blog included criticism of government plans for surveillance of encrypted platforms
- Contributed translations to EFF's HTTPS Everywhere project and advocated for internet freedom
- EFF launches new Offline campaign highlighting his case alongside Ola Bini and Alaa Abd El Fattah
- Demonstrates escalating crackdown on digital rights advocates in Saudi Arabia

## Deep Dive

## Context and Background

Osama Khalid's case represents a disturbing escalation in Saudi Arabia's crackdown on digital rights advocates. Starting as a Wikipedia contributor at age 12, Khalid became a prominent voice for internet freedom, contributing to open source projects including EFF's HTTPS Everywhere while training as a pediatrician. His detention in July 2020 occurred during a broader wave of arbitrary arrests amid COVID-19 lockdowns, suggesting authorities used the pandemic as cover for increased repression.

## Technical and Operational Implications

The case demonstrates how authoritarian regimes are expanding surveillance and persecution beyond traditional activism to target knowledge sharing and documentation. Khalid's "crimes" included Wikipedia edits about human rights abuses and blog posts criticizing government surveillance of encrypted platforms - activities fundamental to maintaining free and open information ecosystems. The arbitrary nature of his sentencing (fluctuating from 5 to 32 to 14 years) reveals the weaponization of judicial systems against digital rights advocates.

## Threat Model for Freedom Tech Builders

This case illustrates how contributing to ostensibly neutral platforms like Wikipedia can be criminalized when they document government abuses. The targeting of someone who contributed to privacy tools like HTTPS Everywhere sends a clear signal about the risks faced by freedom tech contributors in repressive environments. The international nature of Khalid's work - contributing to global projects while documenting local abuses - highlights the borderless nature of both digital rights advocacy and state retaliation.

## Strategic Implications

EFF's decision to highlight Khalid alongside other high-profile cases like Ola Bini and Alaa Abd El Fattah signals recognition that sustained international pressure can shift the political calculus around repression. The case underscores the importance of building robust, decentralized documentation and information-sharing systems that can resist state capture, while also developing stronger international solidarity networks to protect contributors to freedom tech projects.
