# DHS Used 1930s Trade Law to Force Google Data on Canadian Over Anti-ICE Posts

**TL;DR:** DHS leveraged an obscure 1930s trade law to compel Google to surrender data on a Canadian citizen who criticized ICE killings on social media, despite him not entering the US in over a decade.

## Key Points

- DHS targeted Canadian citizen who posted anti-ICE content on X (Twitter)
- Posts specifically condemned killings of Renee Good and Alex Pretti by immigration authorities
- Target has not entered US in more than 10 years, showing extraterritorial reach
- DHS used antiquated 1930s trade law as legal mechanism to compel data surrender
- Google was forced to provide user data including activity and location information
- Case demonstrates cross-border surveillance targeting protected political speech
- Shows how US agencies exploit legal loopholes to circumvent privacy protections
- Highlights vulnerability of centralized platforms to government data demands

## Deep Dive

## Legal Innovation for Surveillance Overreach

This case reveals how DHS is creatively interpreting decades-old legislation to expand surveillance capabilities beyond traditional frameworks. By invoking a 1930s trade law rather than standard mutual legal assistance treaties or national security letters, DHS appears to be testing new legal pathways for cross-border data collection. This suggests systematic exploration of legal loopholes to access foreign nationals' data without triggering normal diplomatic or judicial oversight mechanisms.

## Technical and Jurisdictional Implications

The case demonstrates the vulnerability of users relying on US-based platforms for political expression, even when they are foreign nationals who never enter US territory. Google's compliance indicates that platform terms of service and data localization provide insufficient protection against government overreach. The fact that DHS could obtain both activity logs and location data shows the extensive surveillance capability enabled by modern digital platforms.

## Threat Model Considerations

This represents a significant escalation in how governments can target political dissent across borders using commercial surveillance infrastructure. The use of obscure legal authorities suggests deliberate forum shopping to avoid stronger privacy protections that might apply under conventional legal frameworks. For activists and dissidents, this case proves that criticism of US government actions can trigger surveillance even from outside US jurisdiction.

## Builder Implications

The case reinforces the critical need for decentralized communication infrastructure that cannot be subject to single-jurisdiction data demands. It also highlights how legal creativity by state actors can rapidly outpace privacy protections, making technical solutions more important than regulatory safeguards for protecting cross-border political expression.
