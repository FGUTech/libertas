# Meta Enables Coordinated Attacks on Bangladeshi Protest Archivists Through False Copyright Claims

**TL;DR:** Coordinated attackers are exploiting Meta's copyright system with fake claims to remove Facebook pages documenting human rights violations from Bangladesh's July 2024 uprising, destroying evidence used in war crimes tribunals.

## Key Points

- Community archivists documenting Bangladesh's July 2024 uprising face coordinated takedown campaigns via false copyright claims
- July Revolutionary Alliance lost main page with 547K followers; The Red July lost two pages with 425K combined followers
- Attackers use fake email accounts to file fraudulent copyright claims that Meta accepts without verification
- Targeted content includes evidence being used in Bangladesh International Crimes Tribunal proceedings
- Groups like 'Network-71' publicly announce takedown campaigns, then celebrate successful removals
- Meta fails to act despite clear coordination patterns and explicit threats from political actors
- False claimants impersonate real individuals who confirm they never filed complaints
- Represents systematic digital repression targeting grassroots documentation of state violence

## Deep Dive

## Context and Pattern

This represents a sophisticated weaponization of platform content policies against human rights documentation. The July 2024 Student-People's Mass Uprising in Bangladesh generated significant grassroots archival efforts, with groups like July Revolutionary Alliance (JRA) and The Red July serving as informal but critical repositories of evidence now being used in war crimes proceedings. The coordinated nature of these attacks—including public threats followed by synchronized false copyright claims—demonstrates advanced understanding of how to exploit Meta's automated enforcement systems.

## Technical and Procedural Exploitation

The attacks exploit several weaknesses in Meta's copyright enforcement: acceptance of claims from unverified email addresses, insufficient identity verification of claimants, and inadequate investigation of coordinated abuse patterns. Attackers create fake email accounts (like "julycdi7890@gmail.com" and "shakhawathossain1986@outlook.com"), file claims for content they don't own, and Meta's automated systems remove content without meaningful verification. The speed of enforcement (pages suspended "almost immediately") leaves little opportunity for counter-claims or protective measures.

## Broader Implications for Freedom Tech

This case illustrates how centralized platforms become single points of failure for critical documentation during political transitions. The attacks target not just individual posts but entire archival infrastructure, demonstrating how platform dependency creates systemic vulnerabilities for human rights work. The fact that this content is being used in formal legal proceedings makes these attacks particularly concerning—they represent direct interference with transitional justice processes.

## What Builders Should Consider

This demonstrates the urgent need for decentralized archival infrastructure that cannot be compromised through platform manipulation. The pattern suggests that any single-platform documentation strategy is vulnerable to coordinated abuse of content policies. Future archival efforts need distributed storage, cryptographic integrity proofs, and platform-independent access methods to survive such attacks.
